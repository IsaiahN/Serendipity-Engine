# Scenery & Setting

*The physical world the story inhabits at every scale — from country or region down to the specific room. Scenery is not decoration. It is the medium through which character and theme are made visible. Every setting is always doing something to the story — externalizing interior state, exerting social pressure, carrying history, creating contrast, or earning the reader's belief in the world's reality. A setting that does none of these things is a gap.*

## Scale 1 — World-Level

### Country / Region (Real-World Stories)

*Specify the country, region, and where relevant the city or specific community. Setting is historical and cultural specificity — "Ohio" is not the same as "southeastern Ohio Appalachian hill country," and "1953 Ohio" is not the same as "1953 Alabama." Vagueness at this level is a structural debt — it will cost you later when social rules, infrastructure, landscape, and naming conventions need to be specific.*

*For fantasy or non-real-world settings: skip this section. Build the equivalent under a "World Geography" heading in `world/world-building.md`.*

### Terrain Type

Flat plains / Farmland / Prairie — open sight lines; nowhere to hide; community surveillance is ambient
Rolling hills / Pastoral countryside — enclosed without being trapped; roads are the arteries
Forest — deciduous, broadleaf, seasonal — dense and seasonal; concealment is available and temporary
Forest — coniferous, pine, year-round — dense and permanent; the world before and after human time
Forest — tropical / rainforest — overwhelming life; visibility near zero; the environment as an active force
Mountain range — high and impassable — borders enforced by geography; the world divided
Mountain — foothills and valleys — altitude as social stratification; the high ground is literal and figurative
Desert — hot and arid — scarcity; heat as a constant antagonist; everything is slow or fast with nothing between
Desert — cold and high-altitude — the same scarcity with the additional cost of cold
Coastal — sandy beach — margin between worlds; arrival and departure; the sea as elsewhere
Coastal — rocky cliff — permanence and danger; the sea as something that takes
River delta / Floodplain — fertile and unreliable; the land that changes; built on what came before
Wetland / Marsh / Swamp — liminal; difficult to cross; things that go in don't always come out
Tundra / Arctic — stripped down to essentials; the world without amenity; winter as permanent condition
Volcanic / Lava plain — a world made by violent process; evidence of what the earth is underneath
Urban — dense city — anonymity; proximity without intimacy; the crowd as context
Urban — industrial district — labor; poverty; the material cost of how things get made
Suburban — residential sprawl — conformity as landscape; the world designed to look normal
Small town / Village — everyone knows; exits are visible; return is not the same as belonging
Isolated rural — homestead or farm — self-reliance as necessity; help is far away

## Scale 2 — Climate

Tropical — hot and humid year-round; the body always costs something
Subtropical — warm; distinct wet and dry seasons; the year divided by water
Mediterranean — mild wet winters, hot dry summers; beauty as the dominant register
Temperate / Oceanic — mild, frequent rain, no extremes; grey light as the default
Continental — harsh winters, hot summers; dramatic seasonal swings; the year as a test
Subarctic / Boreal — long winters, short summers; the light itself is a resource
Arctic / Polar — extreme cold; minimal variation; survival as the background condition
Arid / Semi-arid — low rainfall; extreme heat variation between day and night
Monsoon — season of heavy rain followed by dry; the calendar defined by water
Highland — altitude-driven cold regardless of latitude; weather as altitude tax

## Scale 3 — Scene-Level

*Each scene has a specific physical location. Assign it in the chapter outline and use it actively — not as a stage direction but as a story element. The location exerts pressure. The same conversation in a church, a parking lot, and a kitchen are three different scenes.*

### Interior / Exterior

Interior — private (bedroom, home office, kitchen, bathroom, attic, basement)
Interior — semi-private (living room, hotel room, shared office, waiting room)
Interior — institutional (courthouse, school classroom, hospital ward, police station, prison)
Interior — commercial (bar, restaurant, shop, hotel lobby, market stall)
Interior — sacred (church, temple, synagogue, mosque, ritual space)
Interior — transit (train carriage, car interior, ship cabin, bus)
Interior — industrial (factory floor, warehouse, loading dock, back kitchen)
Exterior — private (backyard, garden, farm field, private courtyard)
Exterior — semi-public (street, park, town square, parking lot, schoolyard, bus stop)
Exterior — wilderness (forest, trail, open water, open field, cliff edge, desert road)
Exterior — threshold (doorway, front porch, gate, fence line, border crossing, elevator lobby)

### Time of Day

Before dawn — 2am–5am (the world at its smallest; what people do when they believe no one is watching)
Dawn — first light (threshold; beginning; the world waking; obligations not yet present)
Morning — 7am–11am (routine; ordinary life in motion; the day's work beginning)
Midday — 11am–2pm (the world at full visibility; no shadows; nowhere to hide)
Afternoon — 2pm–5pm (the day's energy winding down; the window before evening)
Late afternoon / Golden hour — 4pm–7pm (warm light; nostalgia; the day's last offer)
Evening — 7pm–10pm (transition; guard coming down; social time; the shift from public to private)
Night — 10pm–2am (private; transgressive; the world after the rules of daylight)

### Light & Weather Conditions

Clear and bright — high visibility; everything is legible
Overcast — flat grey light; no shadows; the world flattened
Fog or mist — partial visibility; edges dissolve; what's out there is unclear
Rain — light and persistent — atmosphere without drama; the world continuing under duress
Rain — heavy and sudden — interruption; the world forcing a pause or a change of plan
Thunderstorm — violence at a distance; pressure made audible
Snow — falling — the world muffled and transformed; visibility narrowing in real time
Snow — settled — post-storm silence; the world changed overnight; the new difficulty of navigation
Wind — notable — not storm; the kind that interrupts; things taken, doors opened
Heat haze — the world distorted; effort at the cost of clarity
Dark — interior low light, nighttime, underground; the body navigating by partial information

## What the Setting Is Doing

*Every scene's setting should be actively performing at least one of these functions. If it is not, the setting is passive — that is a draft problem, not a stylistic choice.*

**Externalizing interior state** — The weather, architecture, or physical detail mirrors or ironizes a character's emotional condition. The fog is not just fog. The locked room is not just a room.

**Exerting pressure** — The setting limits options: who can be here, what can be said, who is watching, what the rules are. A conversation in a public square is governed differently than the same conversation in a private garden.

**Carrying history** — The location has a past that the characters know and the reader doesn't yet, or vice versa. The building is still there after what happened in it. The field is the field where the thing occurred.

**Creating contrast** — The setting's established emotional register (warmth, safety, celebration, sanctity) makes the scene's actual emotional content more acute by operating against it. Violence in a church. Grief at a birthday party. A difficult truth told in beautiful weather.

**Earning belief** — The detail is purely functional: a specific, accurate, observed physical detail that earns the reader's trust that someone has been here. Not decoration — authentication.

## Per-Chapter Scene Metadata (for `outline.md`)

*Each chapter entry in the outline should include a one-line scene specification block. This is structural information, not flavour text — a chapter in which the setting is "somewhere unspecified" is a chapter with a gap in it.*

```
Chapter {N}: {Title}
  Location: {country/region → city/community → specific place}
  Interior/Exterior: {type from list above}
  Time of day: {range from list above}
  Season / temporal context: {season + where we are in the story's calendar}
  Setting active function: {which function from the list above this setting is performing}
```

*Example:*
```
Chapter 3: The Bishop's House
  Location: Wayne County, Ohio → Charm → Bishop Troyer's parlour
  Interior/Exterior: Interior — semi-private (formal reception room, door to kitchen visible)
  Time of day: Morning (9am — a visiting hour; daylight; nothing informal)
  Season / temporal context: Late November 1953 — Advent has not yet begun; the harvest is over
  Setting active function: Exerting pressure (the room belongs to the Bishop; nothing said here is private)
```
