# Time & Calendar

*The story's relationship to time is a structural element, not backdrop. A story that passes through summer into winter has a different shape than one set in a single afternoon. Holidays anchor the reader to shared cultural rhythm, mark the passage of time without announcing it, and generate contrast scenes. How long the story stretches determines what kind of transformation is plausible — a person cannot rebuild a life in three days; they can make one irreversible decision.*

## Story Time Span

*Decide this in Phase 3, before the outline exists. The time span sets the ceiling on transformation — a protagonist cannot change irrevocably in three hours; a marriage cannot dissolve in one afternoon unless it was already gone. The span is a structural commitment.*

A few hours — single day or single night
A few days — under a week
One to two weeks
One month
Several months — under a year
One year
Two to three years
A decade
Multiple decades — generational span
An indeterminate stretch — time marked by events, not calendar
Non-linear — multiple time periods interwoven; the "span" is the total range

## Seasons

### Real-World

Spring — renewal, instability, the world before it settles; mud and possibility in equal measure
Summer — peak and fullness; the illusion of permanence; heat and pressure; nowhere to be cold
Autumn / Fall — decay as beauty; transition; harvest and the end of abundance; the world remembering loss
Winter — scarcity, dormancy, the long wait; what survives when stripped back; forced proximity and isolation

### Fictional / Non-Real-World

*For fantasy, science fiction, or otherworldly settings, the standard four-season cycle need not apply. Define what cycles exist in this world and record in `world/world-building.md`:*

- How many seasons? How are they named?
- Are seasons feared, celebrated, or used as the primary dating system?
- Does a season carry moral or spiritual weight — a "dead season," a "burning time," a period when certain things are forbidden or required?
- Are seasons reliable in this world — or has the natural cycle become unstable, and is that instability part of the story?

## Days of the Week

### Real-World

Monday · Tuesday · Wednesday · Thursday · Friday · Saturday · Sunday

*The specific day of the week matters when the scene's social context depends on it. A conversation on a Sunday morning in a devout community is structurally different from the same conversation on a Wednesday afternoon. A market day, a day of rest, or a day associated with a particular institution (court day, confession, payday) produces pressure without requiring a line of dialogue about it. Assign specific days to key scenes when it earns its cost.*

### Fictional

*For non-real-world settings: define how this world measures a week. Is there a rest day? A market day? A day with ritual significance — one when contracts may not be signed, or the dead may not be buried, or the gates are kept open? Record in `world/world-building.md` if it bears on the story.*

## Holidays & Calendar Markers

### Real-World (English-language / Western default — adjust for world period and cultural setting)

**Winter / Early Year**
Christmas (December 25) · Boxing Day (December 26) · New Year's Eve / Day (Dec 31 / Jan 1) · Epiphany (January 6) · Groundhog Day (February 2) · Valentine's Day (February 14)

**Spring**
Shrove Tuesday / Mardi Gras (moveable, Feb–Mar) · Ash Wednesday (moveable) · Passover (moveable, March–April) · Easter (moveable, March–April) · May Day / International Workers' Day (May 1) · Mother's Day (second Sunday in May, US/UK) · Memorial Day (last Monday in May, US)

**Summer**
Father's Day (third Sunday in June) · Juneteenth (June 19, US) · Midsummer / St. John's Eve (June 23–24) · Independence Day (July 4, US) · Bastille Day (July 14, France) · Assumption (August 15, Catholic)

**Autumn**
Labor Day (first Monday in September, US/Canada) · Rosh Hashanah (moveable, Sept–Oct) · Yom Kippur (moveable, Sept–Oct) · Halloween (October 31) · All Saints' Day (November 1) · All Souls' Day (November 2) · Guy Fawkes Night (November 5, UK) · Thanksgiving (fourth Thursday in November, US; second Monday in October, Canada) · Advent begins (Sunday nearest November 30)

*Note 1: The holiday calendar is always a social argument — it declares who the community considers themselves to be and who they are not. In stories set in specific cultural or religious communities, the community's own calendar takes precedence over this default list. A Mennonite community in 1953 Ohio, a West African household in contemporary London, and a secular Manhattan apartment observe entirely different rhythms. Define the relevant calendar for this story's world.*

*Note 2: Every holiday on this list must pass a context check before use. Ask three questions: (a) **Does it exist yet?** — if the story is set before a holiday was established or widely observed, it cannot appear; roll again. (b) **Does it fit this world?** — a far-future or fantastical setting may not share real-world holidays at all, or may observe distorted derivatives of them shaped by centuries of drift; treat those as new holidays with their own names and meanings, defined in the Fictional framework below. (c) **Does it fit this community?** — a holiday that the story's dominant culture does not observe is not neutral background; its absence, or its presence in a character who does observe it, is a story detail. When a holiday fails the context check, either roll again from the same list or build an equivalent in the Fictional / Community-Specific framework below.*

### Framework for Fictional / Non-Western / Community-Specific Holidays

*If the story's world has its own calendar, or the cultural setting falls outside the Western default, build the relevant holidays here:*

For each holiday, define:
- **Name** — what is it called?
- **What it marks** — seasonal, historical, religious, agricultural, political, or invented
- **Who observes it** — and who is excluded, either by rule or by circumstance
- **What it requires** — fasting, gathering, specific dress, specific roles by gender / age / class / social position
- **What happens at its edges** — who breaks the rules; who enforces them; who watches from outside

## How Holidays Function in Story

*Holidays are structural gifts. Use them deliberately:*

**Time markers** — A scene at Christmas tells the reader exactly where in the year they are and exactly how much time has passed since the last reference point. The holiday does the calendar work without a line of narration.

**Contrast scenes** — The holiday's expected emotional register (warmth, togetherness, festivity, solemnity) set against the actual emotional state of the characters creates resonance without requiring the story to announce it. A character alone on Christmas morning does not need to explain that they are alone.

**Community pressure** — Holidays are moments when society exerts its behavioral expectations. Characters who cannot perform the required emotion, or who refuse to, become visible precisely because the holiday demands conformity. The person not singing, not eating, not celebrating, not praying — is the person the reader watches.

**Arc compression** — The same holiday appearing twice in a story (once early, once late) produces a before/after comparison without requiring transition narration. How the character responds to Christmas in Chapter 2 vs. Chapter 12 is character arc made legible.

**World texture** — A holiday unique to this story's world, observed in passing detail rather than explained, makes the world feel inhabited rather than constructed.

## Story Drift & Seasonal Creep

*Writers rarely write a manuscript in one continuous session. A book written across months or years accumulates the writer's own seasons — the chapter drafted in December carries different atmospheric pressure than the one drafted in July, even if both are set in the same fictional spring. This is normal. It becomes a problem when the drift is invisible; it becomes an asset when it is used.*

**The drift check:** When returning to a draft after a significant gap, re-read the final paragraph of the last chapter written before consulting the outline. Ask: does the register match where the tonal arc says it should be — or has the writer's current emotional season leaked in? Both answers are valid. The question is whether the drift is acknowledged and absorbed deliberately, or allowed to accumulate as accidental noise.

**Using seasonal inspiration:** If inspiration strikes mid-draft from the writer's real season — a winter-inspired scene, a holiday interaction that wasn't in the outline, a weather detail that suddenly feels important — evaluate it against the story's structural needs. If it serves: find where it fits in the outline, plant it deliberately, and make it load-bearing. If it doesn't: note it in a scratch file for another project. The story's designed arc is the contract with the reader. Seasonal inspiration is raw material, not override authority.
