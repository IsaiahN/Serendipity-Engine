# World Building Questions

*Questions to construct the world the story inhabits — its rules, its history, its textures, and its contradictions.*

---

## Rules & Society
- What are the explicit rules of this society — laws, codes, customs?
- What are the punishments for breaking them, and who decides?
- What are the unspoken rules — the things everyone knows but no one says aloud?
- Who enforces the rules? Can they be bribed, corrupted, or circumvented?
- What was once forbidden that is now allowed? What was once acceptable that is now punished?
- What rule exists in this world that would be incomprehensible or monstrous to an outsider?
- **Society as Character:** What does this society claim to protect — and what does it actually protect?
- What is this society's internal contradiction — the thing it cannot acknowledge about itself?
- What is the social pathology it perpetuates: scapegoating, erasure, enforced silence, class exploitation, purity enforcement?
- What does Society demand from the characters who live inside it? What is the cost of membership?
- Who administers Society's rules at the ground level — and what do they personally believe about those rules?
- Is Society stable, fracturing, or already losing its grip when the story begins? What has changed recently?

---

## Power & Government
- What kind of government or power structure does this world have?
- Who holds power, and who is excluded from it?
- How is power transferred — inheritance, election, conquest, divine right, merit?
- Who benefits from the current structure? Who suffers, and do they know it?
- Is the system stable, fracturing, or already collapsing when the story begins?
- Who is trying to change the structure? Who is trying to preserve it?

---

## Environment & Ecology
- What does the physical world look like — geography, climate, landscape? *(See `Story/World Building/scenery.md` for terrain and climate roll lists)*
- What is the relationship between people, animals, and the natural world?
- Are there scarce resources that generate conflict?
- What does the natural world threaten in this world? What does it provide?
- Is the environment itself a character — hostile, indifferent, sacred?
- What does the terrain demand from the people who live in it? (coastal fishing, desert water management, mountain isolation, urban density)
- What does the landscape look like at different times of year — and does the story need to account for that change?

---

## Technology & Infrastructure
- What technologies exist in this world?
- What technologies do not exist that we take for granted?
- How do people travel? How fast? How far can they go?
- How do people communicate across distance?
- Who controls access to information, and who is kept in the dark?
- What does technology make possible — or impossible — for the story?

---

## Culture & Belief
- What do people believe about death, the afterlife, or the divine?
- What myths or founding stories does this society tell about itself?
- What art, music, food, or ritual matters in this world?
- What is considered beautiful? Shameful? Sacred? Profane?
- What does this world celebrate, and what does it mourn?
- What does this culture value above all else — and what does that cost the people in it?

---

## History & Time
- What happened before the story begins that everyone is still living inside of?
- What war, disaster, revolution, or golden age shapes the present?
- What do people in this world misremember or mythologize about their own history?
- What was this world like before — and is that past recoverable, or gone?
- **Story time span:** How long does this story stretch from first scene to last? *(See `Story/World Building/time-and-calendar.md` for span options)*
- Which seasons does the story pass through — and does the seasonal shift carry thematic weight?
- Which holidays or calendar markers fall within the story's span, and what do they mean for the characters and the community? *(See `Story/World Building/time-and-calendar.md`)*
- Is time marked by calendar in this world, or by events, seasons, harvests, rituals — and what does that choice reveal about the culture?
- Does any character experience the same recurring moment (the same holiday, the same season) more than once in the story — and what does that repetition cost them?

---

## Story Justification
- Why must this story be told in this world, and not another?
- What does this setting make possible that no other setting could?
- What truth about the real world does this world reveal, distort, or examine?
- What would be lost if you moved this story to a different time or place?
---

## Cultural Composition & Naming Culture

*Culture is not monolithic in most worlds \u2014 people travel, intermarry, trade, are displaced, and absorb from one another. These questions are not about enforcing a single cultural identity, but about understanding what forces have mixed in this world and what that costs or enables.*

- What cultures, ethnicities, or regional traditions coexist in this world?
- Where do those cultures blend \u2014 in language, food, naming conventions, religious practice, fashion, music?
- Where do they remain separate \u2014 and is that separation enforced, chosen, or simply the result of distance and time?
- Are certain names or naming traditions markers of status, origin, belonging, or exile in this world?
- What happens to someone in this world whose name, appearance, or cultural practice marks them as outside the dominant culture?
- **Monoculture as a theming structure:** If this world is deliberately homogeneous \u2014 a closed community, an isolated colony, a totalitarian state \u2014 that monoculture is itself a story argument. It produces specific mechanics: insularity, purity enforcement, the treatment of outsiders, and the cost paid by anyone who does not fit. If your world is monocultural, name it as a deliberate choice and document it in `world/world-building.md`. A monoculture that exists because cultures were simply not considered is a gap, not a design.
