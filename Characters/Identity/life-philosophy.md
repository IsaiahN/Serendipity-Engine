# Life Philosophy

*A person's core operating worldview — what they believe about existence, meaning, and how to live. Shapes their decisions, their voice, their tolerance for ambiguity, and what they write about or fight for.*

---

> **Author & each character (individually):** Roll 1 from any section. The author's philosophy becomes their unconscious editorial lens — the worldview their story rewards by default. Each character's philosophy is what they believe life is *for*, consciously or not.
> **Foil:** Cross-section foils (Western vs. Eastern, Modern vs. Ancient) produce the widest contrast. Within-section foils are subtler and more unsettling — they share the same vocabulary but reach opposite conclusions: Stoicism ↔ Hedonism, Existentialism ↔ Absurdism, Utilitarianism ↔ Virtue Ethics, Nihilism ↔ Humanism, Rationalism ↔ Mysticism. For protagonist/antagonist pairs, within-section foils work best — they can *almost* understand each other, which is more dangerous than total incomprehension.

## Western Philosophy
Stoicism — control what you can, accept what you can't; emotion is managed, not suppressed
Epicureanism — pleasure (especially simple, lasting pleasure) is the highest good; avoid pain and anxiety
Existentialism — existence precedes essence; meaning is self-created in an indifferent universe
Absurdism — life is meaningless and we keep living anyway; the struggle itself is enough
Nihilism — nothing has inherent meaning or value; can be despairing or liberating
Pragmatism — truth is what works; ideas are judged by their practical consequences
Utilitarianism — the right action produces the greatest good for the greatest number
Hedonism — pleasure and personal enjoyment are the primary goals
Kantianism / Deontology — moral duties and rules matter regardless of outcome
Virtue Ethics — the goal is to become a person of good character, not just to do good acts
Rationalism — reason is the primary source of knowledge and truth
Empiricism — experience and evidence are the only reliable basis for knowledge

---

## Eastern & Spiritual Philosophy
Buddhism — suffering comes from attachment; the path is release and present awareness
Taoism — align with the natural flow of things; effortless action (wu wei)
Confucianism — social harmony through duty, ritual, and right relationships
Hinduism (Advaita / Dharmic) — all is one; fulfill your dharma (duty) in this life
Zen — direct experience over doctrine; clarity through simplicity and presence
Shinto — reverence for nature and the sacred in everyday things
Sufism — love and devotion as the path to divine union
Ubuntu — "I am because we are"; identity rooted in communal belonging

---

## Modern & Personal Worldviews
Humanism — human beings, reason, and compassion are enough; no supernatural required
Secular spirituality — meaning without religion; wonder without doctrine
Optimism — things tend toward good; people are capable of growth
Pessimism — suffering is the baseline; expectations should be low
Cynicism — people are primarily self-interested; idealism is a trap
Fatalism — what will happen will happen; resistance is futile
Determinism — all events are caused by prior events; free will is an illusion
Moral relativism — right and wrong depend on context, culture, and circumstance
Tribalism — loyalty to one's group above abstract principles
Romanticism — emotion, beauty, and individual expression are the highest truths
Minimalism — meaning comes from stripping away the unnecessary
Individualism — the self is sovereign; personal freedom is paramount
Collectivism — the group matters more than any individual
Intersectionality — identity and power cannot be understood in isolation from one another
