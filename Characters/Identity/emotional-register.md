# Emotional Register

*The emotional tone a character or author operates in — their default mood, the hue of their inner world, and the atmosphere they create. A story can shift through several of these, like movements in music.*

---

> **Author (Phase 1):** Roll 1 = the author's default lens. Every scene they write will be slightly tinted by this register, even when writing characters in a different state.
> **Each character (individually):** Roll 1 = their baseline state at the story's opening. This is where they *start*, not where they end — the arc is the phase shift away from and back toward this register.
> **Story tonal arc (Phase 6):** Roll or design a sequence of 3–5 registers as waypoints across the story's acts. This is the signal pattern — the planned interference design. (e.g., Act 1: Tense → Act 2 midpoint: Euphoric false peak → Act 2 end: Desolate → Act 3: Resolute). Each chapter's dominant tone should trace this arc.
> **Foil:** Characters whose baseline registers sit at opposite ends of the spectrum are natural foils: Euphoric ↔ Desolate, Tender ↔ Numb, Joyful ↔ Bitter, Hopeful ↔ Resigned. Same scene will be filtered entirely differently through each of them.

## Primary Registers

**Euphoric** — Overflowing joy, often fragile or manic underneath. Everything feels possible. Sentences run long. Beauty is everywhere. Can tip into delusion or precede a crash.

**Joyful** — Steady, warm happiness. Grounded delight. Not manic — genuinely at ease. Rare in literary fiction; powerful when earned.

**Hopeful** — Forward-looking. Believes things can improve. Not naive — has seen hard things and still reaches toward something.

**Tender** — Soft, gentle, careful with feeling. Close attention to small moments. High emotional sensitivity. Easily wounded.

**Nostalgic** — Oriented toward the past. Finds the present lacking or the past irretrievable. Beautiful and melancholic in equal measure.

**Melancholic** — A deep, quiet sadness. Not despairing — more like a permanent grey cast. Reflective, introspective, slow-burning.

**Somber** — Heavy, serious, weighted. Not sad exactly — more like grief that has been lived with long enough to become gravity.

**Wistful** — Longing for something just out of reach — a person, a time, a version of oneself. Soft ache, not sharp pain.

**Pensive** — Thoughtful, internal, slow. Watches more than acts. Asks questions without expecting answers.

**Anxious** — On edge. Hyperaware of what could go wrong. Sentences may fragment or spiral. Trust is low.

**Tense** — Wound tight. Controlled, but barely. A pressure waiting for release.

**Restless** — Unable to settle. Bored, dissatisfied, hungry for something unnamed. Prone to bad decisions.

**Desperate** — At the end of something. Options narrowing. Risk tolerance high. Capable of anything.

**Angry** — Hot or cold. Righteous or corrosive. Can be the fuel behind action or the thing that burns everything down.

**Bitter** — Old anger that has curdled. Grievance carried so long it has become identity. Sharp edges, closed heart.

**Numb** — Dissociated, flat, checked out. Sometimes a survival strategy. Sometimes a warning sign.

**Despairing** — Believes things cannot improve. Meaning has collapsed. May still function — but the lights are off inside.

**Darkly Humorous** — Finds absurdity in suffering. Laughs because the alternative is worse. Can be coping, connection, or deflection.

**Sardonic** — Dry, detached, quietly contemptuous. Sees through things. Keeps everyone at arm's length with wit.

**Playful** — Light, energetic, delights in the world. Doesn't take itself too seriously. Can hide depth or be exactly what it seems.

---

## Tonal Shifting
*Real writing moves through registers the way a song moves through keys. A story might:*
- Open euphoric and end in grief
- Hold melancholy throughout with one moment of unexpected joy
- Start numb and slowly warm
- Cycle: hope → despair → hope, each time harder won
- Maintain a sardonic surface over a tender core the reader slowly earns access to
