# Character Voice

*How a character speaks — not what they say, but the texture, rhythm, and architecture of their speech. Voice is the output of psychology pressed through language. Every rolled attribute a character has — wound, MBTI, Enneagram, age, background, philosophy — is also a voice instruction. This file makes that translation explicit.*

*Roll after the full character attribute suite is complete. Voice is downstream of everything — it is how all the other rolls become audible.*

*Referenced by: `MetaFiles/Master-Story-Checklist.md` Phase 4 · Phase 8 (Character Consistency Check)*
*Roll for: Author (Phase 1) + every named character (Phase 4). Not rolled for Extras or Catalyst Characters.*

---

> **Per character:** Roll 1 from each section, or derive from existing rolled attributes using the translation notes. The goal is a voice fingerprint — a 4–6 attribute combination specific enough that you can hear who is speaking without a dialogue tag. Two characters should not share the same combination. If they do, rediversify at least two attributes — same voice = merged characters on the page.
>
> **Foil:** Two characters with the same wound but different speech rhythms are the most useful pairing — same damage, different scar tissue, audible on first exchange.

## Speech Rhythm

*The sentence-level architecture. How this character packages thought into language. This is the most distinctive voice marker — readers recognize rhythm before they register word choice.*

Clipped / Staccato — short sentences, few conjunctions, each statement its own unit. Gets to the point. Doesn't backtrack.
Declarative / Direct — complete sentences, subject-verb-object, no ornamentation. Factual delivery even of emotional content.
Deliberate / Measured — moderate length, careful construction, pauses built in. The speaker is aware of the weight of words.
Flowing / Periodic — long sentences with dependent clauses that build before arriving at the main point. The meaning accumulates.
Rambling / Associative — one thought pulls the next; sentences trail into each other; energy over precision.
Fragmented / Trailing — sentences started and left unfinished. Trailing off is a form of statement.
Circling / Recursive — returns to the same idea from different directions. Never states directly what it approaches repeatedly.
Rhythmic / Musical — cadence matters; sentences have a beat; the speaker listens to themselves speak.
Question-forward — speaks through questions more than statements; challenges, invites, unsettles.
Lists / Enumerative — organizes thought into series; finds comfort in the countable; precision as defense.
Run-on / Breathless — connective tissue (and, but, so, because) runs clauses together without full stops; anxiety or enthusiasm.

*Derive from MBTI and Enneagram: Thinking types (T) tend toward declarative or clipped; Feeling types (F) toward flowing or circling. Type 5 Enneagram tends toward precise and sparse; Type 7 toward rambling or run-on; Type 4 toward circling or fragmented.*

## Vocabulary Register

*The level and domain of language this character reaches for. Vocabulary is social history — it tells you where someone has been and what they read, or didn't.*

Formal / Elevated — complete words, no contractions, standard grammar, Latinate vocabulary. Projects authority or education.
Educated Casual — correct but not stiff; switches registers deliberately; comfortable with complexity without displaying it.
Plain / Working — short words, concrete, no abstraction unless necessary. Direct. Mistrusts complexity.
Technical / Specialist — draws from a professional or domain-specific vocabulary; reveals occupation or obsession.
Literary / Reference-dense — quotes, alludes, reaches for classical or canonical language; shows what they've read.
Colloquial / Regional — idiomatic, possibly with regional markers, contractions, local vocabulary.
Slang-forward — current vernacular; age-coded; culture-coded; signals in-group belonging.
Sparse-to-the-point-of-withholding — minimal word count; meaning compacted; makes the other person do work.
Overexplaining / Hedged — qualifiers, clarifications, restatements; more words than necessary; anxiety or precision.
Code-switching — moves between registers depending on who they're talking to; socially adaptive; reveals how they read rooms.

*Derive from Age + Education + Background: A character who left school at 14 and a character with a doctorate are not using the same vocabulary unless one is performing the other's register for a reason — and that performance is itself a voice note.*

## Volume & Pacing Tendency

*How this character inhabits space when speaking. Volume is not just loudness — it is the social claim a speaker makes.*

Loud by default — fills the room without trying; emotion amplifies this further; doesn't always know they're loud.
Quiet by default — makes others lean in; presence established through withdrawal; intensity contained.
Loud under pressure — quiet until something breaks; then volume arrives all at once.
Quiet under pressure — gets softer when threatened or overwhelmed; withdrawal is the defense.
Measured regardless — consistent volume regardless of emotional state; control as a performance.
Rapidly accelerating — starts at normal pace and speeds up as they go; thought outrunning the mouth.
Slowing down to make a point — uses pause and deceleration as emphasis; knows that silence is a weapon.
Halting / Stop-start — pauses mid-sentence, restarts; words require effort or care; or the speaker is hiding.

## Dialogue Tic

*A single recurring speech signature unique to this character. Not a catchphrase — a habit. Used sparingly, it functions as a fingerprint. Overused, it becomes a cartoon.*

*Roll 1, or identify from the character's wound and flaw — a tic is often a psychological mechanism made audible.*

Answers a question with a question
Deflects with humor when the subject is serious
Qualifies everything — "probably," "maybe," "I think," "sort of"
Interrupts, then apologizes, then interrupts again
Starts with "Look —" or "Listen —" when challenged
Uses the other person's name mid-sentence to establish or reassert intimacy
Trails off with "...you know?" requiring the other person to affirm they understand
Repeats the last few words of what was just said to them before responding
Gives an answer to the question before the question was finished
Speaks for others — "what they mean is" / "what you're really asking is"
Changes the subject at emotional peaks rather than completing the feeling
Over-explains rationale when a simple answer would suffice
Uses "we" when they mean "I"
Uses "I" emphatically when most people would soften it
Pauses before names, as if deciding which version of the person to address
Reframes corrections as questions — "Did you mean…?" when they know exactly what was meant
Falls silent exactly when silence is most uncomfortable for others
Swears as punctuation — not from anger but from emphasis or emphasis failure

## Metaphor Family

*The domain of experience this character's analogies, comparisons, and figures of speech are drawn from. A character's metaphor family reveals what they've been immersed in — their occupation, their obsession, or their wound.*

*Roll 1, or derive from Profession + Background + Life Philosophy.*

Sports / Competition — everything is a game, a score, a season, a play
Military / Tactical — strategies, positions, retreats, victories; the world as terrain to be navigated or defended
Religion / Scripture — parables, sin, grace, revelation; meaning is always larger than the event
Food / Hunger — nourishment, appetite, sustenance, waste; the body's wants as moral vocabulary
Weather / Nature — seasons, tides, storms, soil; the world is made of forces, not decisions
Construction / Architecture — foundations, scaffolding, load-bearing, ruin; things are built or they collapse
Medicine / Anatomy — symptoms, diagnoses, healing, infection; everything has a cause and a prognosis
Law / Justice — evidence, verdicts, sentences, appeals; events are argued, not felt
Money / Transaction — investment, debt, interest, cost; everything has a price; nothing is free
Animals / Nature — predator/prey, instinct, pack behavior; humanity is not as elevated as it pretends
Travel / Geography — distance, maps, roads, arrivals and departures; life is movement
Domesticity / Household — the kitchen, the table, the bed; meaning in the ordinary and habitual
Music / Sound — rhythm, dissonance, silence, key changes; things harmonize or they don't
Theater / Performance — roles, masks, audience, exits; the world is a stage and everyone knows their lines

## Defensive Speech Pattern

*What this character's voice does under threat, fear, embarrassment, or grief — before they have time to choose. The defensive pattern is involuntary. It is the wound made audible.*

*Derive from Flaw + Enneagram type shadow.*

Gets louder — volume as authority; aggression preempts vulnerability
Goes quiet — withdrawal as self-protection; makes the other person fill the silence
Deflects with humor — makes the uncomfortable moment into a joke; the laughter is the exit
Over-explains — buries the feeling under reasoning; the explanation is the avoidance
Pivots to someone else's problem — shifts attention outward; the self becomes unavailable
Becomes formal — the emotional register drops; language stiffens; professional distance appears
Attacks first — names the other person's flaw before their own can be named
Goes performatively calm — the affect flattens; the voice becomes controlled; this reads as frightening
Agrees immediately — capitulates to defuse the threat; will return to it later via different means
Asks clarifying questions indefinitely — buys time through apparent interest; never answers
Becomes precise to the point of pedantry — corrects grammar, challenges definitions; makes the conversation about accuracy
Disappears mid-conversation — changes subject, leaves the room, stops responding

## Subtext Default

*What this character cannot say directly. The gap between what they mean and what they say is where their most important moments live. Every character has one thing they consistently route around.*

*Derive from Wound + Life Philosophy.*

Cannot say "I need you" — routes through competence, logistics, or humor instead
Cannot say "I was wrong" — routes through actions, re-framing, or pre-emptive self-criticism about something else
Cannot say "I love you" — routes through service, protection, material provision, or deflection
Cannot say "I'm afraid" — routes through anger, problem-solving, or self-deprecating jokes
Cannot say "I'm proud of you" — routes through critique of what could still be better
Cannot say "I don't know" — routes through confidence, deflection, or redirecting the question
Cannot say "I'm sorry" — routes through fixing the consequences rather than naming the cause
Cannot say "I'm lonely" — routes through busyness, irritability, or insisting everything is fine
Cannot say "Please don't leave" — routes through making themselves useful or difficult to leave
Cannot say "I'm in pain" — routes through describing how the situation is simply unfortunate
Cannot say "I'm angry at you specifically" — routes through general frustration or abstracted complaint
Cannot say "I don't understand" — routes through silence, apparent agreement, or changing the subject

## How Voice Changes Under Arc Pressure

*Voice is not static — it shifts as the character moves through the story. These shifts are structural signals to the reader. The voice arriving at the end should be measurably different from the voice at the opening, even if the change is subtle.*

**Voice indicators of arc movement:**

- **Vocabulary simplifies** under grief or collapse — stripped-down language signals the protective layers coming off
- **Sentence length shortens** under crisis — thought fragments; processing is incomplete
- **Metaphor family shifts** when a character's dominant worldview changes — a character who stops using military metaphors has stopped thinking of the world as a battlefield
- **Defensive pattern appears more frequently** as the stakes increase — the wound is more exposed
- **Subtext default breaks** at the story's critical moments — the thing they cannot say becomes, finally, the thing they say. This is the arc.
- **Dialogue tic disappears or inverts** at the turning point — the reflex that served as armor stops working

*Note in the character's file: at what story beat does the voice shift? What does the shift look like? This is an arc commitment, not a stylistic accident.*

## Voice Differentiation Check

*Run this after all named characters are built. Pull the voice fingerprint for each character — rhythm + vocabulary + tic + metaphor family. If any two characters have three or more matching attributes, their voices will merge on the page.*

- Read the same line of dialogue in two characters' voices. Can you tell who is speaking without a tag?
- Put two characters in a scene together. Within three exchanges, is it clear which speaker is which from voice alone?
- Does the author's rolled voice (from `MetaFiles/writing-prose-styles.md`) subsume any character's voice — are some characters just speaking in the author's prose style? That character has no voice yet.
