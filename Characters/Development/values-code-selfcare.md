# Core Values, Personal Code & Self-Care

*Three distinct but related layers of a character's interior operating system. Values are what they believe matters. Code is how those values translate into behavioral rules — often unexamined survival logic from the wound. Self-care is what they reach for when depleted — which reveals, more honestly than almost anything else, what they actually believe they deserve.*

---

> **Author & each character (individually):** Roll or select 1–2 core values. Then select 2–3 items from Personal Code — these are the behavioral rules this character lives by, consciously or not. Then select 1–2 self-care mechanisms. Constraint: at least one self-care mechanism should be in productive tension with the character's flaw or wound — the thing they reach for may be the thing that perpetuates the damage.
> **Foil:** Two characters with identical values but different personal codes are a productive foil — they agree on what matters and disagree entirely on how to act on it. Two characters with different values but the same self-care mechanism are subtler and stranger — they want different things but restore themselves in the same way.

---

## Core Values
*What this character holds as most important — consciously, even proudly. The value they would defend out loud if asked.*

Achievement / Excellence — being the best; leaving something behind that proves they existed
Loyalty — staying when it costs something; not abandoning the people who need you
Honesty / Integrity — telling the truth even when it damages; refusing the comfortable lie
Justice / Fairness — things should be equitable; imbalance is a form of violence
Freedom / Autonomy — no one owns your choices; the self is sovereign
Security / Stability — safety for yourself and those you love is worth almost any cost
Love / Deep Connection — a life without genuine attachment is not a life worth living
Knowledge / Truth — understanding reality is the highest pursuit; ignorance is not neutral
Courage — doing what is right when it is frightening; refusing to let fear make the decision
Compassion / Empathy — suffering matters; the suffering of others is not background noise
Duty / Responsibility — you do what you said you would; obligations don't expire
Faith / Devotion — something larger than the self gives life its meaning and its framework
Beauty / Aesthetic Order — how things are made and arranged is a form of moral statement
Progress / Innovation — the world can be improved; the present is not the final state
Tradition / Heritage — what was built before us has meaning; abandoning it is a kind of forgetting
Self-Reliance / Independence — you should be able to stand on your own; needing others is a vulnerability
Power / Influence — the ability to shape outcomes is worth having; better to have it than not
Pleasure / Enjoyment — life is for living; joy is not something you earn, it is something you take
Simplicity / Clarity — complexity is usually a form of dishonesty; clear and small is better than grand and obscure
Creativity / Expression — making things that did not exist before is what the self is for
Community / Belonging — the group is the unit; identity is relational, not individual
Protection — keeping safe what cannot protect itself; shielding those who depend on you

## Personal Code
*The behavioral rules this character actually lives by — some consciously chosen, many inherited from survival. These are the lines they do not cross, the commitments they keep even when it costs them, and the reflexive rules they apply without examining whether they still make sense.*

Never betray a confidence — if someone trusted you with it, it goes nowhere
Always finish what you start — leaving things incomplete is a form of failure
Keep your word or don't give it — a promise not meant is worse than silence
Protect those who can't protect themselves — if not you, who
Pay your debts — anything owed must eventually be settled
Never ask for what you won't work for — wanting without earning is weakness
Never let them see you bleed — vulnerability is only for people you are certain of
Put family above everything else — blood obligations are non-negotiable
Never lie to someone who trusts you — betrayal through words is the worst kind
Don't start it, but always finish it — aggression unearned; response justified
Leave no one behind — you don't abandon people, even when staying is costly
Question every authority — the person in charge is not automatically right
If you break it, you fix it — damage you caused is your responsibility to repair
Stay out of other people's business — unsolicited intervention is usually arrogance
Take care of your own first — charity begins at home; external obligations come after
Never rely on anyone you can't survive without — dependency is the same as exposure
Every debt gets paid — accounts do not close until they are settled
Do not apologize for who you are — identity is not a thing you owe anyone an explanation for
The end does not justify the means — how you get there is as important as where you go
The ends justify the means — if the outcome is right, the method is a secondary concern
No one gets a second chance to betray you — forgiveness exists; trust does not reset
Don't take more than your share — excess is a form of theft from everyone else
Say what you mean — performance, charm, and softening are all forms of dishonesty
Keep your suffering private — processing pain publicly is a burden placed on others
Do what must be done, feel it later — action first; grief when the task is complete

## Self-Care & Restoration
*What this character reaches for when they are depleted, frightened, overwhelmed, or in grief. This is not necessarily what they should do — it is what they actually do. The gap between what restores them and what they wish restored them is characterization.*

**Healthy / Restorative**
Solitude — time alone in a quiet space, no task required
Physical movement — running, training, physical labor as thought displacement
Immersion in nature — landscape, silence, the world outside human systems
Creative output — writing, drawing, building, making something that did not exist
Reading or deep engagement with someone else's story
Ritual and routine — cooking, cleaning, gardening; the repetitive task as meditation
Music — listening, playing, or singing without an audience
Sleep and deliberate physical rest
Humor — finding something to laugh at, usually including themselves
Service — helping someone else as a way out of one's own interior
Intellectual engagement — problems, puzzles, learning something new
Physical care — bathing deliberately, grooming, dressing with attention
Prayer or meditation
Meaningful conversation with a trusted person — not venting, but thinking out loud together
Touch or physical closeness with someone they trust

**Ambiguous / Dual-Edged**
Work and productivity — throwing themselves back into tasks to avoid sitting with feeling; restores function but not the underlying wound
Social saturation — being with people constantly; restores surface energy while preventing depth processing
Exercise pushed to excess — punishing the body to quiet the mind; genuinely restorative until it isn't
Confession and venting — can release pressure or can become a loop that rehearses the wound without resolving it
Travel or radical change of environment — breaks patterns but can be avoidance dressed as action
Financial spending — control through acquisition; the ownership feeling that is adjacent to security

**Destructive / Wound-Adjacent**
Alcohol or substance use — numbing is not restoration; the wound is still there when it wears off
Isolation that becomes withdrawal — solitude taken past the restorative point; the world shrinks
Overworking to the point of collapse — production as self-punishment or self-erasure
Seeking validation from unreliable sources — the wound drives them to ask for reassurance from the person least equipped to give it
Recklessness — velocity and sensation as an alternative to feeling; the risk is the point
Control behaviors — reorganizing the environment when the interior cannot be controlled

## Social Self-Positioning
*How this character locates themselves in the social world — relative to other individuals and to the society of the story. This is their working theory of their own significance. It is almost always partially wrong.*

**Orientation upward** — Primarily aware of those above them in the hierarchy (more powerful, more admired, more successful). Their energy goes toward gaining recognition from those they consider above them, or toward closing the gap.

**Orientation downward** — Primarily aware of those who need protecting, helping, or leading. They define themselves by their responsibility to others. Can be genuine compassion; can be a way of avoiding being seen as an equal by anyone.

**Orientation lateral** — Primarily aware of peers and rivals. Status is measured by comparison — what others in the same tier have, what they lack, where the line is. Competitive, or deeply aware of fairness, or both.

**Invisible by choice** — Does not locate themselves in any hierarchy because they have opted out of being seen. May be survival instinct (safety in invisibility) or genuine indifference to social ranking.

**Overestimated position** — Believes themselves higher in the hierarchy than others actually place them. The gap between their self-perception and their social reality is a source of friction, comedy, or tragedy.

**Underestimated position** — Believes themselves lower in the hierarchy than others actually place them. Their influence, value, or significance is not something they have integrated into their self-image yet.

**Outsider by nature** — Does not feel they belong to any group or tier. May be an observer, a critic, a feral intelligence that the social world has never fully domesticated. Valuable and lonely in equal measure.

**Insider who secretly doesn't belong** — Occupies a recognized social position but does not feel entitled to it. The imposter syndrome runs deep. Performance of the role is exhausting because they believe the role is not truly theirs.
