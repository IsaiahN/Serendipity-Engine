# Relationships — Questions Answered

*Decomposed from: The Wonderful Wizard of Oz (1900)*

---

## Q: What is the central relationship of the story — and how does it function structurally?

**Dorothy ↔ Companions (collective)**

This is technically four relationships but they function as one structure: Dorothy at the center, companions rotating around her, each companion a mirror of one specific thing she is also missing or denying.

The structural function is a *companion-as-mirror* formation — a recognized narrative technique in which characters who join the protagonist are not simply allies but reflections of the protagonist's unresolved internal questions. Dorothy needs to see Scarecrow discover his brains, Tin Woodman acknowledge his heart, and Lion name his courage — because each discovery is a rehearsal for the revelation she cannot make about herself.

**The key structural question:** Are the companions' journeys about them — or about Dorothy? The text genuinely supports both readings. The companions' arc resolutions (their kingdoms, their recognized qualities) are complete and self-contained. But they are also mirrors. This is the story's most graceful piece of architecture.

## Q: Analysis of each major relationship pair

### Dorothy ↔ Scarecrow

**Type:** Immediate attachment; the first companion relationship formed
**Dynamic:** Dorothy accepts Scarecrow immediately and completely without requiring proof of his intelligence. This is the story's first evidence that Dorothy's practical loyalty is unconditional — she does not wait for the Scarecrow to demonstrate his worth before valuing him.
**Asymmetry:** The Scarecrow's devotion to Dorothy is more intense than any other companion's. He says explicitly that he will stay with her; when she must return home, the departure is his greatest loss.
**Dramatic function:** The Scarecrow's arc demonstrates the theme in its purest intellectual form. He is the most articulate companion about his own supposed deficiency and the most reliably intelligent character in the cast. His plans consistently succeed. The gap between his self-assessment and his demonstrated ability is the story's primary example of the theme.
**Pivot scene:** Ch. 3 — the Scarecrow explains he cannot get lost because he has no brain to direct him wrong, then immediately solves a navigation problem.

### Dorothy ↔ Tin Woodman

**Type:** Protective friendship; the second companion relationship formed
**Dynamic:** Dorothy oils the Tin Woodman. This is the most explicitly physical act of care in the story — she literally enables him to move again. The relationship begins as a service exchange and deepens into genuine mutual affection.
**Asymmetry:** The Tin Woodman's care for Dorothy is protective — he worries about her safety in ways the Scarecrow does not. He is physically capable of fighting, and he does, for her.
**Dramatic function:** The Tin Woodman's arc demonstrates the theme in its most emotional form. He is the companion whose supposed deficiency (no heart) is most visibly contradicted by his behavior (weeps frequently, cares for all living things, mourns loss). The velvet heart in a tin box works because the Tin Woodman needed permission to acknowledge what he already felt — not the feeling itself.
**Pivot scene:** Ch. 5 — Tin Woodman tells his backstory and weeps. He immediately apologizes for weeping, saying he has no heart. He is weeping during the apology.

### Dorothy ↔ Cowardly Lion

**Type:** Protective reciprocity; the third companion relationship formed
**Dynamic:** Dorothy slaps the Lion when he threatens Toto. The Lion, who is much larger and physically more powerful than Dorothy, is stopped cold by a small girl protecting her dog. This is the relationship's founding scene and its essence: Dorothy models the courage the Lion is seeking without knowing she is doing so.
**Asymmetry:** The Lion's respect for Dorothy is tinged with awe — he recognizes in her the quality he most lacks (acting without permission, from pure protectiveness). Dorothy is not afraid of him, which means more to the Lion than he says.
**Dramatic function:** The Lion's arc demonstrates the theme in its most theatrical form — he performs his cowardice as clearly as he performs his courage. His arc is behavioral before it is psychological: he acts courageously throughout the story and only at the end is given permission to name what he was doing as courage.
**Pivot scene:** Ch. 6 — Dorothy slaps the Lion; the Lion weeps and calls himself a coward; the journey continues.

### Dorothy ↔ The Wizard

**Type:** False mentor relationship
**Dynamic:** Dorothy believes absolutely in the Wizard's power. This belief is the engine of Act 1 — it is what moves the journey from Munchkin Country to the Emerald City. The Wizard's exposure is the story's central plot reveal and its deepest thematic statement: the authority you believe holds your answer does not, and the answer was yours to access all along.
**Asymmetry:** The Wizard is afraid of Dorothy from the moment she arrives. She is the first person with a genuine demand he cannot deflect. He stalls her (sends her to kill the Witch), she completes the task (accidentally), and then the curtain is pulled.
**Dramatic function:** The Wizard is the story's critique of institutional authority. His relationship with Dorothy is the story's argument that the authority we defer to may not have what we need — and that this discovery, while disorienting, is liberating rather than catastrophic.
**Pivot scene:** Ch. 15 — Toto pulls back the curtain; the Wizard is revealed; Dorothy says "You're a very bad man"; the Wizard says "I'm a good man, but I'm a very bad Wizard."
**The line that holds:** "I'm a good man, but I'm a very bad Wizard." This is the story's most compassionate treatment of institutional failure — it refuses to make the Wizard a villain. He is ordinary, afraid, and doing the best he can with what he has. He is not the enemy. He is just not the answer.

### Dorothy ↔ Glinda

**Type:** True mentor / resolution relationship
**Dynamic:** Glinda is everything Dorothy believed the Wizard was. She has the knowledge, the power, the genuine benevolence. The difference is that Dorothy arrives at Glinda having already completed the journey — she is ready to receive the truth.
**Asymmetry:** Glinda knows everything and feels nothing she needs to protect. Dorothy knows almost nothing and has only just become capable of going home. The asymmetry is functional: Glinda is the end-state Dorothy is growing toward.
**Dramatic function:** Glinda's relationship with Dorothy raises the story's most unresolved question: did she know from the beginning? If yes, did she withhold the silver shoes' power cruelly or wisely? The text implies she knew and was wise. A generation of feminist critical readings argues she was cruel. Baum's text does not fully resolve this — which is its most interesting moment of authorial restraint.
**Pivot scene:** Ch. 23 — Glinda reveals the silver shoes' power; Dorothy says goodbye to her companions; she clicks her heels three times; she is home.

### Scarecrow ↔ Tin Woodman

**Type:** Intellectual-emotional complementarity; the story's most balanced dyad
**Dynamic:** The Scarecrow thinks; the Tin Woodman feels. Together they cover what the other lacks. They treat each other with warm collegial respect — there is no competition between them, which is unusual for a story that is fundamentally about coveted qualities.
**Dramatic function:** Their complementarity is the story's argument that wisdom and love are not competing values but complementary ones. The story never chooses between them.

### Tin Woodman ↔ Cowardly Lion

**Type:** Emotional mirroring; the story's most tender companion pair
**Dynamic:** Both are repressing their defining quality. The Tin Woodman weeps for a crushed beetle and calls himself heartless. The Lion trembles during a charge into the Witch's castle and calls himself a coward. They are each other's clearest evidence.
**Dramatic function:** Their foil relationship demonstrates that self-knowledge and self-recognition are not the same thing. Behavior does not automatically translate into identity.

### The Wizard ↔ Glinda

**Type:** Counterpart — false vs. genuine authority
**Dynamic:** They never meet on the page. But their juxtaposition is the story's structural spine. The Wizard is in the center of Oz; Glinda is at the edge. The Wizard was the first destination; Glinda is the last. The Wizard cannot help; Glinda can. The Wizard is ordinary pretending to be magical; Glinda is magical and does not need to pretend.
**Dramatic function:** Their relationship demonstrates what the story thinks power looks like. Power is not the impressive center. It is the quiet edge that holds everything together.

## Q: What is the story's relationship structure type?

**Primary structure:** Hub-and-spoke

Dorothy is the hub. All relationships run through her. The companions relate to each other primarily in the context of supporting Dorothy's journey — their relationships with each other are warm but secondary.

**Exception:** Scarecrow ↔ Tin Woodman (active relationship independent of Dorothy) and Tin Woodman ↔ Lion (active relationship independent of Dorothy) create a partial mesh structure within the companion group.

**Network Theory implication:** The hub-and-spoke structure is the story's primary Seven Story Deaths vulnerability (Monolith — all narrative passes through one character). This is intentional in a children's adventure story. The risk is acknowledged: if Dorothy is removed, the narrative collapses. The story accepts this risk as a genre feature.

---

## Q: How does relationship development track across the story?

All companion relationships follow the same trajectory:
1. Meeting (Dorothy extends care immediately and unconditionally)
2. Journey (companions demonstrate their quality while denying they have it)
3. Test (the companion's quality is required and deployed under pressure)
4. Reward (the Wizard's ceremony — a symbol for what was always real)
5. Resolution (each companion receives a kingdom; Dorothy returns home)

The trajectory is identical for all four companions. This consistency is a strength (the thematic argument is hammered into every relationship) and a mild weakness (predictability erodes surprise). The story compensates by differentiating the companions in register and texture.

## Q: Are there significant relationships that are NOT with Dorothy?

Yes — two:

**The Wizard ↔ The Wicked Witch of the West (off-page):**
The Wizard has been afraid of the Wicked Witches for his entire reign. His fear of them is the reason he sent Dorothy to kill the Witch — he was using her as a weapon against someone he lacked the power to confront. This off-page relationship is the story's most morally troubling dynamic.

**Winged Monkeys ↔ Silver Shoes (systematic):**
The Winged Monkeys are bound to the silver shoes. Whoever holds the shoes can command them three times. The Wicked Witch used her three commands. Dorothy uses one (to cross the desert). The shoes are more than a transportation device — they are a power object that changes hands as the story's power hierarchy shifts.

---

## Q: Does any relationship change the character who is NOT Dorothy?

**Yes — and this is the story's most generous design choice.**

The Scarecrow, Tin Woodman, and Lion all receive their kingdoms at the end. Their relationship with Dorothy changed their trajectory. They would not have received recognition without the journey — and they would not have made the journey without her. Their endings are not incidental; they are the story's argument that the help you offer genuinely transforms the person you help AND the person they help along the way.

The Wizard also changes: he departs, which is a change — he has been stationary in the Emerald City for decades. Whether his change is growth or escape is left unresolved.
