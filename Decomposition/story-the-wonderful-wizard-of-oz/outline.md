# Outline — The Wonderful Wizard of Oz

*Chapter-by-chapter decomposition. 24 chapters. Each entry captures: chapter goal, dominant tone, active/resolved subproblem threads, scene metadata, setting function.*

---

## Chapter 1 — The Cyclone

**Chapter goal:** Establish the ordinary world (Kansas); execute the inciting incident (the cyclone carries Dorothy to Oz)
**Dominant tone:** Earnest-melancholy → sudden displacement
**Active threads:** None yet — story has not begun
**Opened threads:** Thread 1 (get Dorothy home)

**Scene metadata:**
- Location: Kansas prairie — Uncle Henry's farm
- Interior/exterior: Both — exterior prairie, interior farmhouse, interior cyclone cellar (partial)
- Time of day: Afternoon
- Season: Late spring / tornado season
- Setting function: **Baseline contrast** — the grey, stripped world of Kansas is rendered in detail so that Oz's vivid strangeness will register as a complete world-reversal. Every specific detail (the cookstove, the baked grey land, Aunt Em's grey face) is designed to establish what Dorothy is escaping from. Setting is working at maximum contrast-building capacity.

**Key evidence from text:**
"The sun had baked the plowed land into a gray mass, with little cracks running through it. Even the grass was not green... she could see nothing but the great gray prairie on every side." The grey repetition is deliberate structural work. Uncle Henry "never laughed." Aunt Em "never smiled now." Toto is the only non-grey thing: "a little black dog, with long silky hair and small black eyes that twinkled merrily." The contrast is total.

---

## Chapter 2 — The Council with the Munchkins

**Chapter goal:** Orient Dorothy in the new world; establish the quest (reach the Emerald City, find the Wizard); plant Chekhov's Gun (silver shoes)
**Dominant tone:** Wonder
**Active threads:** Thread 1 (get home — now has a stated path: find the Wizard)
**Opened threads:** None new; thread 1 gains direction

**Scene metadata:**
- Location: Munchkin Country, Oz
- Interior/exterior: Exterior — the field where the house landed
- Time of day: Morning (Dorothy has slept through the flight)
- Season: Oz has no specified season — perpetual mild
- Setting function: **World revelation** — Oz is introduced as the visual and tonal opposite of Kansas. "There were lovely patches of greensward all about, with stately trees bearing rich and luscious fruits. Banks of gorgeous flowers were on every hand." Every sentence is a deliberate reversal of the grey Kansas opening.

**Key evidence from text:**
The silver shoes are introduced here: "There is some charm connected with them; but what it is we never knew." Chekhov's Gun planted in Chapter 2. Will not fire until Chapter 23. This is a 21-chapter delayed Chekhov's Gun — among the most patient in children's literature.

---

## Chapter 3 — How Dorothy Saved the Scarecrow

**Chapter goal:** Introduce the Scarecrow; establish the theme of "lacking what you already have" through the first companion
**Dominant tone:** Warm-comic
**Active threads:** Thread 1 active; Thread 2 opened (companions seeking their qualities)
**Opened threads:** Thread 2 (Scarecrow's quest for brains)

**Scene metadata:**
- Location: Yellow brick road — cornfield section
- Interior/exterior: Exterior — fields, road
- Time of day: Daytime
- Season: Mild
- Setting function: **Character introduction through contrast** — the Scarecrow is literally in a cornfield, which is a Kansas-adjacent setting. His dislocation from a natural role (scaring crows) mirrors Dorothy's dislocation from Kansas. The setting rhymes.

**Key evidence from text:**
"Do you think if I go to the Emerald City with you that Oz would give me some brains?" The Scarecrow's first creative problem-solving (the river crossing, the forest path) begins almost immediately. He is demonstrating brains while asking for them. This is the story's thematic engine introduced in its first companion encounter.

---

## Chapter 4 — The Road Through the Forest

**Chapter goal:** Narrative progression; world-building of forest Oz; establish that Oz contains genuine dangers
**Dominant tone:** Earnest-adventurous
**Active threads:** Threads 1 and 2 active
**Scene metadata:**
- Location: Forest section of the yellow brick road
- Setting function: **Obstacle and atmosphere** — the forest introduces the idea that Oz is beautiful and dangerous simultaneously. The threat is latent but present.

---

## Chapter 5 — The Rescue of the Tin Woodman

**Chapter goal:** Introduce the Tin Woodman; add the second variation on the theme of lacking what you already have
**Dominant tone:** Warm-comic with undertone of melancholy (the Tin Woodman's backstory is genuinely sad)
**Active threads:** Threads 1 and 2 active; Thread 2 expanded (now includes Tin Woodman's heart)
**Opened threads:** Thread 2 expands (heart)

**Scene metadata:**
- Location: Forest — clearing where Tin Woodman stands rusted
- Setting function: **Backstory container** — the rusted woodman frozen in place is a physical externalization of his condition: he has been stationary (emotionally frozen, literally immobile) since losing his heart.

**Key evidence from text:**
The Tin Woodman's full backstory is delivered here: enchanted axe cut off his limbs one by one, replaced with tin, finally cut out his heart. He was to marry a Munchkin girl but without a heart the desire faded. He is literally hollow at the center of himself. His wound is among the most specific in the story: not metaphorical lack but a structured removal by an external agent (the Wicked Witch's curse).

**Note:** The Tin Woodman weeps when a beetle is crushed near him — immediately after stating he cannot feel. His Stream A and Stream B are in direct conflict from his introduction.

---

## Chapter 6 — The Cowardly Lion

**Chapter goal:** Introduce the Cowardly Lion; complete the companion trio; add the third variation on the theme
**Dominant tone:** Comic (the Lion is the most inherently comedic character — his self-dramatizing shame is played for warm humor)
**Active threads:** Threads 1 and 2 fully established
**Opened threads:** Thread 2 expanded (courage)

**Scene metadata:**
- Location: Deep forest
- Setting function: **Expected habitat, wrong occupant** — a lion in a forest should be the king of his domain. This lion is afraid of everything. The setting underscores his displacement from his rightful identity.

---

## Chapters 7–8 — The Journey to the Great Oz / The Deadly Poppy Field

**Chapter goal:** Advance the journey; deliver the poppy field danger (near-death for Dorothy and Lion); demonstrate group problem-solving (the mice rescue)
**Dominant tone:** Earnest-tense → quick recovery to bright
**Active threads:** Threads 1 and 2 active
**Notable:** The poppy field is the story's closest approach to genuine danger with lasting consequence. Dorothy and the Lion nearly die. Baum resolves it quickly (the field mice queen commands her subjects to drag them out) and returns to the bright road tone immediately. This is the tonal management choice most characteristic of his authorial style.

**Scene metadata:**
- Location: The great poppy field
- Setting function: **Hidden danger in beauty** — the most beautiful setting in the road section is also the most lethal. This is the story's one genuine example of the fairy tale's "beautiful = dangerous" logic.

---

## Chapters 9–10 — The Queen of the Field Mice / The Guardian of the Gates

**Chapter goal:** Resolve the poppy crisis; arrive at the Emerald City; introduce institutional power (the Guardian requires green spectacles)
**Dominant tone:** Wonder (Emerald City approach)
**Active threads:** Threads 1 and 2 active; Thread 3 implicitly opened (what is the Wizard actually?)

**Scene metadata:**
- Location: Road to Emerald City → City gates
- Setting function: **False grandeur** — the Emerald City is described as magnificent, dazzling, overwhelming. Everything is green (including the mandatory spectacles that make everything look green). The spectacles are the story's first direct signal that what you see in Oz depends on what you're made to wear. The Wizard's world is literally a colored lens.

---

## Chapters 11–12 — The Wonderful City of Oz / The Search for the Wicked Witch

**Chapter goal:** Dorothy's audience with the Wizard (each companion separately); Wizard imposes condition (kill the Witch); the group departs west
**Dominant tone:** Gothic-tense → resigned-determined
**Active threads:** Threads 1, 2, and 3 at critical
**Notable:** This section contains the story's most dramatically sophisticated structural move. The Wizard appears to each companion differently:
- To Dorothy: a Great Head
- To Scarecrow: a beautiful woman
- To Tin Woodman: a terrible beast
- To Lion: a ball of fire

Each manifestation is tailored to each character's fear response. This is a detail that rewards adult re-reading — the Wizard is perceptive enough to know what will most intimidate each of them. He is not merely a fraud; he is a skilled performer.

**Scene metadata:**
- Location: The Wizard's throne room, Emerald City
- Interior/exterior: Interior — vast, dark, intimidating
- Setting function: **Institutional performance** — the throne room is designed to amplify the Wizard's apparent power. Size, darkness, and theatrical props all serve to overawe visitors. The room is the Wizard's primary tool.

---

## Chapters 13–15 — The Rescue / The Winged Monkeys / The Land of the West

**Chapter goal:** Journey to the Wicked Witch's castle; the Witch captures Dorothy; Dorothy is enslaved
**Dominant tone:** Sustained tension — the story's most constrained tonal section
**Active threads:** Thread 2 at critical (companions captured or scattered); Thread 1 at risk

**Scene metadata:**
- Location: Winkie Country (yellow), the Witch's castle
- Setting function: **Oppression made physical** — the castle is where the enslaved Winkies are worked, where the Winged Monkeys are commanded. The yellow of Winkie Country is the story's most ambiguous color — it should be cheerful but in this context it is associated with forced servitude.

**Key evidence from text:**
The Witch sees Dorothy's silver shoes and immediately understands their power, even if Dorothy doesn't. "But she dared not harm Dorothy in any way, for she did not know whether her new slave... knew of the wonderful power the silver shoes contained." The shoes create a protection Dorothy is unaware of — the irony of the story's central Chekhov's Gun.

---

## Chapter 16 — The Discovery of Oz, the Terrible

**Chapter goal:** Dorothy kills the Wicked Witch (accidentally, with water); companions are freed; second major milestone
**Dominant tone:** Shock-matter-of-fact → relief
**Active threads:** Thread 2 approaching resolution; Thread 1 still active

**Key evidence from text:**
"I didn't mean to kill her," Dorothy says. This is important. Dorothy does not kill the Witch in an act of heroism or vengeance. She throws water on her in an act of anger (the Witch stole one of her shoes) and the Witch dissolves. The killing is accidental. Baum refuses to make Dorothy a killer-hero. The violence has no weight. This is the authorial choice that most clearly expresses his stated intent to remove "disagreeable incident."

---

## Chapters 17–18 — How the Balloon Was Launched / Away to the South

**Chapter goal:** Return to Emerald City; the Wizard's exposure as a fraud; the Wizard's departure by balloon; the group must find another way home for Dorothy
**Dominant tone:** Deflation → problem-reconstitution
**Active threads:** Thread 2 fully resolved (Wizard's symbolic grants); Thread 3 resolved (Wizard exposed and departed); Thread 1 in crisis (the promised solution has failed)

**Key evidence from text:**
"I am just a common man... I have been making believe." The Wizard's confession is complete. He is Oz by accident. He built the Emerald City out of survival necessity. He has lived in terror of the Witches (who have real magic) and maintained his fraud because exposure would mean death. His motivation is humanized: he is not malicious, he is frightened. This makes his fraudulence more interesting than simple villainous deception.

**Scene metadata:**
- Location: Throne room → launch site
- Setting function: **Spectacle dismantled** — the throne room that housed the terrible manifestations is now just a room. The balloon that was supposed to take Dorothy home leaves without her. The setting underscores the failure.

---

## Chapters 19–21 — Attacked by the Fighting Trees / The Dainty China Country / The Lion Becomes the King of Beasts

**Chapter goal:** Resume journey south to Glinda; episodic obstacles (fighting trees, china country, the great spider); the Lion earns his kingdom
**Dominant tone:** Bright-adventurous — the story finds its road energy again after the Wizard's deflation
**Active threads:** Thread 1 active; Thread 2 fully resolved; new micro-thread (Lion's kingdom)

**Scene metadata:**
- Location: Forest → China Country → Great Forest
- Setting function: **Escalating strangeness** — the china country is the story's most surreal single setting. Tiny china people living in a tiny china world, terrified of being broken. It is beautiful, fragile, and poignant — a world that cannot be touched without being damaged. The thematic resonance (things made of beauty are easily destroyed) is the story's subtlest moment.

---

## Chapter 22 — The Country of the Quadlings

**Chapter goal:** Arrive in the South; approach Glinda's castle; the Hammer-Heads obstacle
**Dominant tone:** Warm-anticipatory
**Active threads:** Thread 1 at its final approach
**Scene metadata:**
- Location: Hill of Hammer-Heads → Quadling Country
- Setting function: **Final obstacle before resolution** — the Hammer-Heads are the last physical barrier between Dorothy and the answer. The Winged Monkeys, used for the final time, carry the group over. The red world of the Quadlings is described as rich and happy — warmth earned by contrast with everything that came before.

---

## Chapter 23 — Glinda The Good Witch Grants Dorothy's Wish

**Chapter goal:** The story's true climax — the silver shoes' power revealed; Dorothy chooses home; companions receive their kingdoms
**Dominant tone:** Revelatory warmth — the story's most emotionally open section
**Active threads:** Thread 1 resolved; Thread 2 resolution formalized; Companion future-thread opened and resolved in same chapter

**Key evidence from text:**
"Your Silver Shoes will carry you over the desert. If you had known their power you could have gone back to your Aunt Em the very first day you came to this country." This is the Chekhov's Gun firing after 21 chapters and is also the story's thematic statement: *you had the power from the beginning.*

"But then I should not have had my wonderful brains!" — Scarecrow
"And I should not have had my lovely heart." — Tin Woodman
"And I should have lived a coward forever." — Lion

The companions acknowledge that the journey was necessary — not because Dorothy needed to find external power, but because the journey was the mechanism by which all of them discovered what they already had. The delay of the silver shoes was not a mistake; it was the story. This is among the most elegant structural payoffs in all of 19th-century American literature.

---

## Chapter 24 — Home Again

**Chapter goal:** Dorothy's return to Kansas; the final image
**Dominant tone:** Homecoming warmth — pure, unguarded
**Active threads:** All resolved
**Scene metadata:**
- Location: Kansas prairie — Uncle Henry's new farmhouse
- Setting function: **Mirror of opening** — the final scene is Kansas again, but Dorothy is no longer grey. The grey world has the same grey world, but Dorothy is changed within it. The setting does not change. She changes in relation to it. The homecoming is complete.

**Key evidence from text:**
"My darling child! Where in the world did you come from?" / "From the Land of Oz. And here is Toto, too. And oh, Aunt Em! I'm so glad to be at home again!" — The final line. No explanation of Oz. No attempt to process what happened. Pure homecoming. The story ends in an embrace.
