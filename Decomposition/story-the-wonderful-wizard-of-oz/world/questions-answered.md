# World Building — Questions Answered

*Decomposed from: The Wonderful Wizard of Oz (1900)*

---

## Q: What kind of world does this story take place in?

**Primary genre world:** Portal Fantasy — a secondary world accessed from the primary world through an involuntary transition (a tornado). The reader's anchor world (Kansas, 1900) is real and grey and impoverished. The secondary world (Oz) is vivid, magical, and structurally organized.

**Key structural feature:** The transition is one-way until the silver shoes are understood. Dorothy does not know how to return. This is the generative tension of the story's entire engine — she is in a world she did not choose, seeking a way home, without knowing the way home was always with her.

---

## Q: How does the world reflect the story's theme?

The two worlds are mirror expressions of the theme:

**Kansas:** A world where qualities are suppressed, not developed. The grey flatness is not neutral — it is actively deadening. Aunt Em's laugh has been trained out of her. Uncle Henry works without cease. The land does not allow beauty. Kansas is the world in which Dorothy's capability cannot be seen — not because it does not exist, but because the environment has no use for it.

**Oz:** A world where qualities are visible but misunderstood. The Scarecrow's intelligence is visible to everyone except the Scarecrow. The Tin Woodman's heart is visible to everyone except the Tin Woodman. The Wizard's powerlessness is visible to everyone except the people inside the Emerald City. Oz shows things clearly — but its characters are all looking in the wrong direction.

The theme: both worlds are structured around the gap between what is visible from the outside and what can be acknowledged from the inside.

## Q: What is the world's social structure — and does it function as a character?

**Oz's social structure:**
- Feudal magical hierarchy: each of the four geographic quadrants (North, South, East, West) is ruled by a witch (good or wicked)
- The Emerald City at the center, ruled nominally by the Wizard
- Beneath the rulers: enslaved or bound populations (Winkies enslaved by the Wicked Witch of the West; Winged Monkeys bound by the golden cap)

**The social structure as character:**
The world of Oz is built on a lie at its center and on two ongoing systems of bondage at its edges. The Emerald City is beautiful — and is literally an illusion (green glasses force everyone to see it as emerald). The Winkies work for the Wicked Witch under compulsion. The Winged Monkeys are bound to serve whoever holds the golden cap.

Dorothy disrupts all of this without planning to:
- She kills the Wicked Witch of the East (freeing the Munchkins)
- She kills the Wicked Witch of the West (freeing the Winkies and releasing the Winged Monkeys)
- She exposes the Wizard (dismantling the central illusion)

The world's social structure is the story's argument about power: systems built on compulsion and illusion cannot sustain themselves. Dorothy's presence — an honest person from outside the system — dissolves them.

## Q: What are the world's distinct regions and how do they function?

| Region | Color | Ruled by | Population | Function in story |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Kansas | Grey / Brown | Nobody (federal) | Farmers | The anchor world; the destination and departure point |
| Munchkin Country (East) | Blue | Wicked Witch of East (now dead) | Munchkins | The landing point; the first evidence of Dorothy's disruptive power |
| The Yellow Brick Road | Yellow | — | — | The path; a world structure that makes navigation explicit |
| Emerald City (Center) | Green (illusory) | The Wizard (now gone) | Citizens wearing green glasses | The false destination; the site of the central reveal |
| Winkie Country (West) | Yellow | Wicked Witch of West (now dead) | Winkies | The crisis point; where Dorothy is captured and kills the Witch |
| South Country | Red | Glinda | Quadlings | The true destination; where the real answer is held |
| The Forest | Dark | Nominally the Lion (post-story) | Wild creatures | The test zone for the Lion; the landscape of fear |

**World-building technique used:** Color-coding of geographic zones. This is Baum's most elegant world-building tool — the reader can track Dorothy's position by the dominant color of the landscape. [CONFIRMED from multiple chapter descriptions]

## Q: Does the world have consistent internal rules?

**Rules that hold:**
- Magic is real but bounded — it can do specific things and cannot do others
- The silver shoes' power is always available but requires knowing the correct use
- The golden cap commands the Winged Monkeys three times and then is spent
- Good witches and wicked witches are distinct categories with distinct geographic domains
- The Wizard has no magical power — this rule holds retroactively throughout the entire story

**Rules that are inconsistent:**
- Why does Glinda wait to reveal the shoes' power? This is the world's most significant rule-gap. If she knew, why not tell Dorothy at the beginning? The rule is either (a) the shoes require the journey first, or (b) Glinda's guidance philosophy forbids shortcuts. Neither is explicitly stated.
- Why could Dorothy not use the shoes to escape the Witch's castle? This is not addressed. [INFERRED: the shoes require the wearer to know how to use them; Dorothy did not know]

## Q: What are the world's most important physical locations?

**1. The tornado:** Not a place but the engine of everything. The tornado is the world's transition mechanism — involuntary, violent, displacement as narrative birth. [Ch. 1]

**2. Dorothy's house (as weapon):** The silver shoes are found on the feet of the Wicked Witch of the East, crushed under Dorothy's falling house. A house — the symbol of home — becomes the instrument of the first liberation. [Ch. 2]

**3. The Yellow Brick Road:** The world's only reliable navigation tool. A road that asks you to follow it without knowing where it goes. Its color (yellow = wealth, gold, the promise of something better) and its explicit direction (follow it) make it the story's most concrete external structure. [Ch. 2 onwards]

**4. The Emerald City:** The center of the world's illusion. Beautiful, imposing, built on glasses and performance. Its function in the story is to be impressive and then to be revealed as hollow. [Ch. 9–18]

**5. The Witch's castle:** The story's darkest physical space. Dorothy is enslaved here; the companions mount a rescue; the Witch is killed. The castle is the world's site of genuine menace — the only place in Oz where the story's stakes become real. [Ch. 14–16]

**6. Glinda's palace:** The world's true resolution space. It is described as red and beautiful and warm. Everything the Emerald City pretended to be, Glinda's palace is. [Ch. 23]

## Q: What does the world's economy look like?

**Kansas:** Explicit subsistence farming. No surplus. Grey and difficult. [CONFIRMED: "When Dorothy stood in the doorway and looked around, she could see nothing but the great gray prairie on every side."]

**Oz:** No currency or exchange economy is described. Characters are given what they need (oil for the Tin Woodman; food for Dorothy and Toto) without exchange. This is either a gift economy or an economy-free secondary world — Baum does not specify.

**Symbolic reading:** The absence of economic exchange in Oz may be Baum's implicit contrast to Kansas. In the grey economic world, everything is scarce and earned. In the magical world, what you need arrives when you need it. The story never interrogates this contrast, but it is present.

---

## Q: What sensory vocabulary does the world use?

**Sight:** The dominant sense. Oz is a visual world — every region has a distinct color palette; every major character has a distinct visual signature; every major location is described in explicit visual terms.

**Sound:** Secondary. The tornado roars; the Witch's castle has sounds of menace; the companions' footsteps on the road are occasionally described.

**Touch:** Appears at moments of physical care (Dorothy oiling the Tin Woodman; Dorothy carrying Toto; Dorothy stroking the Lion). Touch marks relationship.

**Taste/Smell:** Nearly absent. This is appropriate for a fairy-tale register — fairy tales are primarily visual and tactile, not olfactory.

## Q: Seven Story Deaths audit for the world-building

**Monolith (all motion through one character):** ACTIVE. All of the world's major events require Dorothy's presence. The Wicked Witches were not killed before Dorothy arrived. The Wizard was not exposed before Dorothy arrived. Nothing resolved without her. The world is genuinely Monolithic.

**Amnesia (world forgets its past):** PARTIAL. We learn very little about Oz before the Wizard arrived. The world has a past (the witches have been there; the populations existed) but it is not shown. The Wizard's decades-long performance is the only backstory given texture.

**Hierarchy (power flows only downward):** ACTIVE but deliberately exposed and subverted. The story's whole arc is the dismantling of the false hierarchy (Wizard → Glinda → Dorothy → powerless companions). By the ending, the hierarchy is inverted.

**Monopoly (one type of solution):** PARTIAL. Every problem in the story is solved by Dorothy's presence or action, which creates a monopolistic solution structure. However, the companions solve things independently (Scarecrow's plans, Lion's courage, Tin Woodman's axe-work), which partially distributes the solution pool.

**Isolation (characters cannot see each other's inner lives):** ACTIVE but by design. No character can see that the other characters already have what they seek. This Isolation is the story's engine — without it, the journey would have ended in Chapter 3.

**Monoculture (world has only one kind of person):** ACTIVE by genre requirement. Oz is structured to provide a distinct new challenge every few chapters, but the moral framework is binary (good vs. wicked) and the population types are simple (Munchkins, Winkies, etc.). This is a fairy-tale world, not a complex one.

**Stasis (world cannot change):** PARTIAL. The world changes as a result of Dorothy's journey (Munchkins freed, Winkies freed, Wizard gone, companions made rulers). The world is not static — but it required an outside agent (Dorothy) to initiate all change. Without her, it would have remained static indefinitely.

**Closure (story wraps everything up):** FULLY ACTIVE. Every story thread resolves. Every character receives a destination. Dorothy returns home. The Wizard departs. Only one thread is genuinely unresolved: what happens to the Wizard after he departs in the balloon, and the silver shoes are lost in the desert. Baum chose not to close these — either oversight or intentional reserve for sequels.
