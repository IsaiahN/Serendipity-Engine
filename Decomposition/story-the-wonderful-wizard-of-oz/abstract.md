# Abstract — The Wonderful Wizard of Oz

---

## Title

**The Wonderful Wizard of Oz**
*Working title was also the final title. Published 1900. L. Frank Baum.*

---

## Short Description

A Kansas orphan named Dorothy is swept by a cyclone into the magical Land of Oz, where she must follow the yellow brick road to find the great and terrible Wizard who can send her home — picking up three companions along the way, each convinced they lack the one quality they need. The central question: do the things we most desperately seek already live inside us — and if so, why can't we see them?

---

## Abstract

*The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* is a story about the gap between what we believe we lack and what we actually possess. Dorothy doesn't need the Wizard to get home; she had the power all along. The Scarecrow is the wisest member of the group; the Tin Woodman is the most loving; the Lion is the bravest. None of them can see this because each is locked inside a belief about themselves that their origin story produced — the Scarecrow was told he was worthless by crows, the Tin Woodman lost his heart to an enchantment, the Lion cannot reconcile his fear with his idea of what courage means. The Wizard, who claims to be the source of all these qualities, is exposed as an ordinary man using machinery and spectacle to maintain an illusion of power. The story's deepest argument is that institutional authority — the great and terrible Oz, the voice behind the curtain — has no real power to give you what you need. You already have it. The cost of discovering this is the journey: the friendships made, the dangers survived, the growing evidence that these characters have been demonstrating their qualities all along. The story ends with homecoming, not triumph — which is the correct ending for a story whose argument is that the real world, grey and small and ordinary, is worth coming back to.
