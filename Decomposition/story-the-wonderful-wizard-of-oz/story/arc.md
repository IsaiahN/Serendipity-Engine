# Story Arc — The Wonderful Wizard of Oz

*Decomposed from all 24 chapters. Arc is described as it exists in the finished text, not as it would have been designed in advance.*

---

## Protagonist Arc

**Dorothy Gale — Arc Type: Identity Confirmation (not transformation)**

Dorothy does not change her fundamental self across the story. This is unusual and worth noting. She does not learn to be brave or wise — she already was. What changes is her understanding of her own situation: she learns that she had the power to go home from the beginning.

Her arc is therefore not a traditional growth arc but a **recognition arc** — a story in which the protagonist discovers who they already are, rather than becoming someone new.

| Beat | Protagonist's State | Subproblem Stack |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | Lost, disoriented, grieving for home. Practical and calm despite fear. | Problem 1 active: get home |
| Ch. 3–6 | Still grief-for-home as baseline; becoming competent navigator of strange world. Loyalty developing. | Problem 1 active; Problem 2 opened: companions need their qualities granted |
| Ch. 11–12 (Wizard meeting) | Trust in authority figure (Wizard). Accepts his condition without question. | Problem 1 on hold; Problem 2 restructured: must kill Witch first |
| Ch. 12–16 (Witch's castle) | Captured. Most vulnerable point. Grief and danger at peak. | Problem 2 at critical; Problem 1 at risk |
| Ch. 17 (Witch's death) | Empowered by accidental action. The Witch dissolves from a bucket of water. | Problem 2 resolved (companions get their qualities) |
| Ch. 18 (Wizard exposed) | Betrayal/disillusionment. Wizard cannot help. | Problem 1 in crisis — the promised solution was a fraud |
| Ch. 22–23 (Glinda's revelation) | Recognition. The power was always there. The silver shoes. | Problem 1 resolved |
| Ending | Returns home. The arc is complete: she knows she is capable, she knows where she belongs. | All problems resolved |

**The arc in one sentence:** Dorothy moves from displaced girl who believes power is elsewhere to a girl who discovers the power was in her all along — and chooses home.

---

## Subproblem Stack

**Primary thread:** Get Dorothy home to Kansas
- Opened: Chapter 1 (cyclone)
- Sustained: Entire story
- At critical: Chapter 18 (Wizard's failure)
- Resolved: Chapter 23 (Glinda's revelation + silver shoes)

**Secondary thread:** Companions receive their requested qualities (brain, heart, courage)
- Opened: Chapters 3–6 (each companion joins and states their need)
- Sustained: Act 2
- At critical: Chapters 15–16 (Witch's capture — companion arc nearly extinguished)
- Resolved: Chapter 17 (Witch's death) + Chapter 18 (Wizard's symbolic grants)
- Complication: The grants are revealed to be symbolic — the qualities were always present

**Tertiary thread:** The Wizard's fraud / the question of authority
- Opened implicitly: Chapter 11 (the Wizard's terrifying manifestations raise the question of who this person actually is)
- Made explicit: Chapter 18 (the little man behind the screen)
- Resolved: Chapter 18 (the Wizard departs; authority is revealed as empty performance)
- Residue: Glinda remains as the one authority figure who is genuine

**Quaternary thread (Companions' post-story futures)**
- Opened: Chapter 23 (Glinda grants each companion a kingdom to rule)
- Resolved: Chapter 23 (same chapter — quick closure)

## Tonal Arc

*The authorial stance toward the material, distinct from Dorothy's interior emotional arc.*

```
Opening tone:         Earnest-with-melancholy (Kansas sections). The grey world is rendered directly,
                      without sentimentality but with clear affection for the people trapped in it.
                      The tone is warm toward Aunt Em even in her greyness.

Tone at Act 1 end:    Wonder (the arrival in Munchkin Country). The color, the flowers, the Munchkins,
                      the Witch of the North — Baum's tone opens up completely. The story becomes
                      genuinely delighted with itself.

Tone at Act 2 mid:    Comic-earnest (the road through Oz). The episodic encounters (Kalidahs,
                      poppies, river crossing, Winged Monkeys) are dangerous but the tone never
                      tips into genuine dread. Baum keeps the forward movement bright.

Tone at Act 2 end:    Earned tension (the Witch's castle). This is the story's most constrained
                      section tonally — Baum maintains warmth but the danger is real enough that
                      the earnestness is under pressure. The closest the story comes to sadness.

Closing tone:         Homecoming warmth. The final image (Aunt Em's embrace, "my darling child")
                      is the story's emotional argument: joy is possible, and it lives at home.

Deliberate departures:
  - The Wizard's manifestations (Ch. 11–12): Brief gothic foreboding — the Great Head,
    the beautiful woman, the beast, the ball of fire. The tonal departure produces the
    story's only moment of genuine menace. It is immediately resolved.
  - The poppy field (Ch. 8): A sudden near-death that is tonally out of register with
    the rest of the journey. The poppies produce sleep, not pain — but the threat is real.
    Baum resolves it quickly (the mice rescue). Tone returns to earnest-bright immediately.
  - The Witch's death (Ch. 16): Brief shock — the witch simply dissolves. The tone is
    matter-of-fact, almost comic. "I didn't mean to kill her." The matter-of-factness is
    the tone managing the violence: if you don't dwell on it, it isn't terrible.
```

**Dominant authorial tone throughout:** Earnest
The story means what it says. The wonder is real wonder. The homecoming is a real homecoming. The love Dorothy's companions feel for her is treated as genuine and significant. There is no ironic distance between Baum and his material. This is the engine and the limitation.

## Tonal Differentiation

**Author tone:** Earnest — warmth, delight in the world's strangeness, conviction that the journey matters

**Narrator tone:** Conversational earnest — slightly warmer and more directly engaged with the reader than a purely formal narrator. The narrator is clearly having a good time.

**Character tones (distinct from narrator):**
- Dorothy: Practical-earnest. Worried, competent, specific. She frets about Aunt Em's feelings about mourning clothes. She notices silver shoes might fit. She is not dreaming — she is managing.
- Scarecrow: Cheerful-philosophical. Light about his own deficiencies. Curious. Invents solutions. Voice is slightly more intellectual than Dorothy's.
- Tin Woodman: Formal-tender. Speaks carefully. Registers emotion with restraint. The most emotionally precise voice in the group.
- Cowardly Lion: Verbose-self-deprecating. Dramatic about his shame. Overexplains his inadequacy. Performs cowardice while performing courage simultaneously.
- Wizard: Grandiose → deflating. The gap between his public register (thundering, terrifying) and his private register (apologetic, ordinary) is his character's entire arc compressed into a reveal.
- Glinda: Measured warmth. Knows more than she says. Gentle but precise.

## Phase 6 Verification (Retrospective)

**Ending-first design check:**
The ending (Dorothy clicks her heels three times and returns to Kansas, landing in the arms of Aunt Em) is the correct ending for this story. Every detail flows from it: the silver shoes must be established early (Chekhov's gun), the Wizard must fail (false resolution, forcing the second arc to Glinda), and the silver shoes' power must be withheld until the last possible moment (Glinda's revelation). If the ending had been "the Wizard sends Dorothy home," none of the thematic weight would exist. The ending is the argument: the power was always yours.

**Tonal arc alignment with author's Big Picture Statement:**
The tonal arc confirms the Big Picture Statement exactly. The story opens in the wound (grey Kansas) and closes in the answer (the warmth of homecoming). The arc is: grey → wonder → danger → revelation → warmth. The author's stated intent — wonder and joy retained, heartache left out — is structurally delivered. The heartache of the Kansas opening is real but brief. The wonder is sustained across nearly the entire story. The closing warmth is the payoff Baum was building toward.
