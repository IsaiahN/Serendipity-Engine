# Characters — Questions Answered (Cast-Level)

*Decomposed from: The Wonderful Wizard of Oz (1900)*

---

## Q: What is the cast's complete network archetype distribution?

The Serendipity Engine framework identifies four archetypes: Pioneer, Optimizer, Generalist, Exploiter.

| Character | Archetype | Role in Cast |
|---|---|---|
| Dorothy Gale | Pioneer | Sets the journey in motion, disrupts every power structure she touches without intending to, drives all forward momentum |
| Scarecrow | Optimizer | Devises solutions, improves the group's practical capability, receives knowledge and uses it efficiently |
| Tin Woodman | Generalist | Bridges emotional and practical registers, provides care and physical protection, translates between Scarecrow's logic and Lion's emotion |
| Cowardly Lion | Generalist | Provides physical protection, grounds the group in physical reality, demonstrates courage-without-name |
| The Wizard | Exploiter | Extracts belief and labor from the Oz system without contributing genuine magical value; uses Dorothy and companions as tools for his own safety |
| Glinda | Optimizer / Anchor | Maintains world stability, enhances function of all characters she touches, reveals the resolution Dorothy could not access without the journey |

**Coverage:** All four archetypes are present. The cast double-covers Generalist (both Tin Woodman and Lion serve bridging functions for different registers). The Exploiter role is explicitly exposed within the story — the Wizard's extraction is not normalized; it is the story's central reveal. This makes the Network Theory analysis structurally clean.

## Q: What are the foil pairs in the cast?

The story operates on systematic mirroring. Every major companion is a foil to Dorothy (same wound, different expression), and the companions foil each other.

**Dorothy ↔ The Wizard:**
Both are ordinary people from the American Midwest (Kansas, Omaha) who arrived in a strange world by accident. Dorothy stayed honest about who she is; the Wizard built a fiction about who he is. Dorothy returns home; the Wizard's departure is uncertain. This is the story's deepest foil: the same premise producing opposite choices and opposite fates.

**Dorothy ↔ Each Companion (shared wound, different expression):**
- All four companions are denying a quality they already possess (Scarecrow: brains; Tin Woodman: heart; Lion: courage). Dorothy is denying the power she already has (silver shoes). All five are in the same fundamental condition, but Dorothy is not aware she is in it.

**Scarecrow ↔ Tin Woodman:**
Brains vs. Heart. Intellect vs. Emotion. The Serendipity Engine framework would describe these two as covering complementary halves of what a fully realized person requires. When the story poses the question "What matters more — thinking or feeling?" it refuses to answer, because both demonstrate their quality persistently while denying they have it.

**Tin Woodman ↔ Cowardly Lion:**
Heart vs. Courage. Tender vs. Fierce. Both are denying the quality they most persistently demonstrate. Both require external authority (the Wizard's ceremony) to accept the quality they have always had. Their foil relationship is the story's most emotionally resonant double.

**The Wizard ↔ Glinda:**
False authority vs. genuine power. Performance vs. substance. What people think they need vs. what actually helps them. The Wizard gives the companions what they need (permission, symbol) but cannot do what he claimed. Glinda gives Dorothy what she needs (the truth she was ready to hear) and actually has the power she appeared to have.

## Q: Collision check — do any two characters share the same want + need + wound simultaneously?

A collision would create a redundant character — two characters competing for the same psychological space.

| Character | Want (Surface Goal) | Need (Deep Goal) | Wound |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dorothy | Return home to Kansas | Self-recognition of her own capability | Displacement and orphanhood |
| Scarecrow | Gain a brain | Permission to trust the intelligence he already has | Told he was worthless; internalized mockery |
| Tin Woodman | Gain a heart | Permission to acknowledge the love he already feels | Literal enchantment: heart removed, love faded |
| Cowardly Lion | Gain courage | Permission to call "acting despite fear" courage | Never told that fear and courage can coexist |
| Wizard | Maintain his reign / return home | Acceptance of his own ordinariness | Identity consumed entirely by an accidental performance |
| Glinda | N/A (already resolved) | N/A | N/A (or wound never shown) |

**Result:** No collisions detected. Each character occupies a distinct psychological position:
- Dorothy's wound is about belonging
- Scarecrow's wound is about intellectual worth
- Tin Woodman's wound is about emotional capacity
- Lion's wound is about self-definition of courage
- Wizard's wound is about identity fragility

Their wants partially overlap (Wizard and Dorothy both want to go home) but the mechanism, psychological driver, and narrative function are entirely different. The overlap is thematically intentional — not a collision, but a mirror.

## Q: How does the companion structure distribute the story's philosophical weight?

The companions are not just travel partners. They are Dorothy's externalized internal questions:

**Scarecrow = "What does wisdom look like?"**
Answer the story gives: Wisdom looks like a stuffed man who keeps proposing better solutions than anyone else and never credits himself for them.

**Tin Woodman = "What does love look like?"**
Answer the story gives: Love looks like a tin man who weeps over a crushed beetle and insists he cannot feel.

**Lion = "What does courage look like?"**
Answer the story gives: Courage looks like a lion with shaking knees who shows up anyway.

**All three together:** The story is arguing that the qualities we most need are visible in our behavior, not in our self-perception. The Wizard can give symbols but not substance; Glinda can name what is already there; but the companions had to demonstrate it over the course of an entire journey before the revelation would land.

## Q: What is the power hierarchy of the cast — and how does it invert?

**Opening hierarchy (appearance):**
1. The Wizard — feared, absolute, grants wishes
2. The Wicked Witches — powerful, territorial
3. Glinda — mentioned but distant
4. Companions — seeking power from authority
5. Dorothy — a child with nothing

**Closing hierarchy (reality):**
1. Glinda — genuine power, genuine knowledge, genuine goodness
2. Dorothy — has always held the power of return; destroyed two Wicked Witches (accidentally)
3. Scarecrow — now rules the Emerald City
4. Tin Woodman — now rules the Winkies
5. Lion — now rules the Forest
6. The Wizard — gone; his reign shown to have been hollow from the start

The inversion is complete. The apparent center of power (the Wizard) was the Exploiter. The apparent ordinary person (Dorothy) was the Pioneer holding the key. The apparent nobody companions are now the rulers.

## Q: What are the character arc types in the cast?

| Character | Arc Type | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Dorothy | Recognition arc | Discovers what she already has; does not become someone new |
| Scarecrow | Recognition arc | Discovers what he already has; does not become someone new |
| Tin Woodman | Recognition arc | Discovers what he already has; does not become someone new |
| Cowardly Lion | Recognition arc | Discovers what he already has; does not become someone new |
| The Wizard | Evacuation arc | Is found out; departs; his arc is not transformation but exit |
| Glinda | No arc | She is already the destination; she does not journey |

**Note:** All four companions share the same arc type — Recognition. This is not a collision but a thematic statement. The entire story is about a single question (do you already have what you need?) and every main character is an answer to that question. The consistency is architectural.

## Q: What is the story's largest character design risk — and does it succeed?

**Risk:** Having five protagonists (Dorothy + four companions) where four are essentially answering the same question creates the risk of redundancy and repetition.

**How the story avoids it:**
1. Each companion's domain (wisdom, love, courage, Dorothy's belonging) is genuinely distinct.
2. Each companion's wound-mechanism is distinct (external mockery, literal enchantment, no external wound, displacement).
3. Each companion's voice is distinct in register and tempo.
4. The companions are introduced sequentially across multiple chapters, so each gets a full chapter of establishment before the group forms.
5. The companions' foil relationships with each other (Scarecrow ↔ Tin Woodman, Tin Woodman ↔ Lion) create genuine interpersonal texture.

**Verdict:** The design risk succeeds. The repetition is structural, not experiential.
