# Glinda, the Good Witch of the South — Character Decomposition

*Decomposed from: The Wonderful Wizard of Oz (1900)*

---

## Identity

**Age Range:** Ageless — described as young and beautiful despite being the most powerful witch in Oz. This is typical of Baum's construction of good female authority. [CONFIRMED from Ch. 23]

**Gender:** Female [CONFIRMED]

**Religion / Faith:** Not stated. Her authority in the story is closer to the divine than any other character — she is the one person in Oz who holds genuine power and knows everything. She functions as the story's closest analogue to a benevolent deity: present at the beginning and end, guiding rather than controlling, allowing suffering rather than preventing it for the purpose of growth. [INFERRED]

**Life Status:** Ruler of the South Country and the Quadlings. The most powerful of Oz's witches. Has records of everything that happens in Oz — explicitly stated to possess a Book of Records that updates in real time. [CONFIRMED from Ch. 23]

**Life Philosophy:** Guidance through constraint — the most helpful thing you can do for someone is not to solve their problem for them, but to ensure the journey that solves it for them actually happens [INFERRED]
This is the most controversial element of Glinda's character: she knew from Chapter 2 (or could have known) that the silver shoes had the power to take Dorothy home. She chose not to tell Dorothy until Dorothy had traveled the entire length of Oz, lost companions, watched the Wizard fail, journeyed to the South, and was ready to hear it. Whether this is wisdom or cruelty is genuinely unresolved by the story.

**Emotional Register:** Measured warmth — she is kind but entirely unruffled; she answers every question directly; she never shows uncertainty or distress [CONFIRMED from Ch. 23]
Glinda's emotional register is the opposite of Dorothy's turbulence and the Lion's emotional volatility. She is the still point. This reads as genuine equanimity rather than suppression — she simply operates at a different register than the people she helps.

## Personality

**MBTI:** INFJ-A (The Advocate — Assertive) [INFERRED]
INFJs are principled, visionary, quietly strategic, and deeply committed to doing what is right — even when it is costly. They see long patterns that others miss. They are rarely distressed by what they know is coming because they have already processed it. Assertive INFJ: confident in their vision, not shaken by challenge, present in service of others without losing themselves. Glinda knows Dorothy has the power to go home. She withholds this knowledge strategically. She does this because she has calculated that the journey is necessary — not punitive, but formative. This is a deeply INFJ decision.

**Enneagram:** 1w2 (The Reformer — wing Helper) [INFERRED]
Type 1s are driven by the conviction that there is a right way to do things and that their responsibility is to uphold it. Glinda is the story's only figure of genuine moral authority — she operates within a world where everything else is disordered (the Wizard is fraud, the Wicked Witches are cruel, the Emerald City is literally a palace of illusions) and maintains order by actually being what the Wizard only pretended to be. Her wing 2 is her helpfulness — she is not a cold, purely principled person; she genuinely cares for the people who come to her. But she helps by guiding rather than rescuing.

**Moral Alignment:** Lawful Good [CONFIRMED from consistent behavior]
Glinda does what is right, as she understands right. She does not improvise, bend rules for convenience, or allow pragmatism to override principle. She helped the Munchkins the moment the Wicked Witch died. She helped Dorothy the moment she appeared. She answered every question fully. She is the most reliable actor in the story.

## Development

**Character Type:** Optimizer / Anchor [CONFIRMED from story function]
Glinda maintains the world's stability. She is the backstop — when the Wizard fails, the journey does not end; it reroutes to Glinda. When Dorothy arrives, Glinda has everything already known and understood. She does not discover the solution; she reveals it. She is also an Optimizer in the network sense: she enhances the function of everyone she touches, including giving the Scarecrow her throne room as a departure point and helping the Tin Woodman and Lion receive their kingdoms.

**Physical Description:** Young in appearance, beautiful, dressed in white, gentle in manner. Her palace is described as rich and beautiful. She is visually coded as the true counterpart to the Wicked Witches: same category (witch), opposite in every expressive quality. [CONFIRMED from Ch. 23]

**Core Flaw:** Withholds the silver shoes' power for the entire story — she knows, and she does not tell [INFERRED — this is the story's most discussed interpretive problem]
The text does not explicitly confirm that Glinda knew from the beginning. It is possible she needed Dorothy to bring the shoes to her before she could understand their full power. But the story strongly implies that Glinda is the one character with complete knowledge. If she knew, her decision not to tell Dorothy immediately is the biggest act of withholding in the narrative.

The interpretation: Glinda understood that Dorothy could not use the shoes until she had done the work of the journey. She needed to know she was capable. She needed to help her companions discover themselves. She needed to be the person who arrived at Glinda's door — not the frightened child who arrived in Munchkin Country. The shoes require faith that home is where you belong. Dorothy at Chapter 2 did not have that faith yet.

Whether this is wisdom or cruelty: the story says wisdom. Modern interpretations frequently say cruelty. Both readings are supported by the text.

**Core Virtue:** Principled guidance — she helps in the way that produces lasting change rather than temporary relief [CONFIRMED from story function]

**Core Wound:** None shown. Glinda is the closest the story gets to a figure without visible wound. This is intentional — she is the story's figure of resolution, not transformation. She is what the world looks like when things have been set right. [INFERRED — Baum was not writing her as fully human; she is closer to a narrative function than a character in the psychological sense]

**Stream A vs. Stream B:**
Glinda does not demonstrate an observable Stream A / Stream B conflict. She has already resolved her internal conflict, if she ever had one. This is what makes her functionally different from every other character: she is not on a journey. She is the destination.

**Network Archetype:** Optimizer (also the story's Anchor — the point of stability around which all resolution organizes)

**Foil:** The Wizard (genuine power vs. sustained fraud; substance vs. performance; true authority vs. manufactured authority)
**Foil:** Dorothy (adult, omniscient, settled vs. child, learning, displaced; what Dorothy might eventually become, translated through experience and growth)

**Voice Fingerprint:**
- Speech Rhythm: Complete sentences, no hedging, no self-qualification. She is the inverse of every other character's dialogue pattern. Where the Scarecrow qualifies everything and the Lion undercuts himself, Glinda states.
- Vocabulary Register: Formal, warm, clear. She does not condescend and does not perform knowledge. She simply has it.
- Dialogue Tic: Directness. "The silver shoes have a wonderful power" — she explains immediately, completely, without drama.
- Metaphor Family: None detectably. She does not need metaphor; she speaks directly to the thing.
- Defensive Pattern: She has none. She is not defending anything.
- Subtext Default: *You always had everything you needed. I am here to make that visible.*

**Post-story fate:** Remains as ruler of the South. She continues to be what she has always been. The story does not need to resolve her further; she was already resolved. [CONFIRMED — she appears only in Ch. 23 and the story ends shortly after]
