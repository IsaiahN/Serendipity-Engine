# Narrator Decomposition — The Wonderful Wizard of Oz

*Decomposed from prose evidence across all 24 chapters.*

---

## Narrator Type

**Third Person Omniscient — Reliable** [CONFIRMED from text]

The narrator has access to the inner thoughts, feelings, and perceptions of multiple characters throughout the story — but most consistently follows Dorothy. The narrator is not a character in the story. They have no stake in events. They are a trusted adult guide standing just outside the story, speaking to the reader with calm authority.

Evidence: The narrator freely reports Dorothy's inner states ("Dorothy felt as if she were going up in a balloon"), the Scarecrow's self-perception, the Tin Woodman's emotional history, and even the Cowardly Lion's private shame. No single character's perspective limits what the narrator can access. When Glinda knows something the reader needs to know, the narrator reports it directly.

---

## Reliability

**Reliable — with one notable structural exception** [INFERRED]

The narrator is reliable in the strict sense: they do not deceive the reader, misreport events, or withhold information for the narrator's own agenda. What they report is what happened.

The single structural exception is the Wizard of Oz himself. The narrator presents the Wizard's various manifestations (the great head, the beautiful woman, the beast, the ball of fire) as Dorothy and her companions perceive them — terrifying and enormous. The narrator does not break ranks to tell the reader that this is all illusion. This is a reliable narrator maintaining dramatic suspense through selective emphasis rather than deception. The information is withheld from the reader the same way it is withheld from Dorothy — the reveal is a shared discovery.

This is the most sophisticated narrative move in the book. The narrator is technically reliable throughout, but chooses to ride Dorothy's ignorance about the Wizard, creating a gap between narrator knowledge and narrator disclosure. The gap is the story's one genuine twist.

---

## Perspective

**Third Person Omniscient, dorothy-anchored** [CONFIRMED from text]

Grammatically third person. Omniscient in access. But the story never actually leaves Dorothy for more than a few sentences. The narrator can access any character but chooses, by strong preference, to remain close to Dorothy's experience. This is not third person limited — the narrator can and does step outside Dorothy — but Dorothy is the center of gravity.

The narrator is not peripheral or observational. They have full interiority access. "Dorothy soon closed her eyes and fell fast asleep" — the narrator knows when Dorothy sleeps. "She felt quite lonely" — the narrator reports interior emotional states as fact.

---

## Tense

**Past tense** [CONFIRMED from text]

"Dorothy lived in the midst of the great Kansas prairies." The story is fully past tense, told as if recounting something that has already happened. This creates the "story told at bedtime" register that Baum was explicitly cultivating. The past tense positions the narrator as someone who has already seen the whole of it and is now, calmly, showing it to you.

---

## Distance

**Close — narrator as trusted adult guide** [CONFIRMED from tone]

The distance is close but not intimate in the confessional sense. The narrator does not confide in the reader or break the fourth wall. But the voice is warm and direct — "the little girl gave a cry of amazement" — and never clinical or detached. The narrator clearly likes Dorothy. This affection is not stated; it is structural. Everything the narrator chooses to linger on — Dorothy's practical preparations for the journey, her care for Toto, her specific worry that "Aunt Em will surely think something dreadful has happened" — reveals an author and narrator who find this small girl worth watching carefully.

---

## Narrator vs. Author Gap

**Minimal gap — author and narrator are nearly aligned** [INFERRED]

Baum's stated authorial intent (wonder without fear, joy without punishment) and the narrator's voice are nearly identical. The narrator is not a mask in the way an unreliable or ironic narrator would be. The narrator believes what Baum believes. The narrator wants what Baum wants — for the reader to feel wonder and then safety.

The only gap worth noting: Baum the biographical man knew grief, failure, and financial fear. The narrator of this book does not. The narrator's emotional equanimity is a performed version of what Baum wanted the world to look like, not a description of how Baum himself experienced it. The narrator is Baum's aspirational self — the adult who has found equanimity — not his lived self.

---

## Narrative Voice & Tone

**Conversational + Earnest** [CONFIRMED from text]

The prose speaks to the reader as a trusted adult speaks to a child: with clarity, warmth, without condescension, and without irony. Events are described accurately and directly. The narrator never signals that the reader is being naive for caring about what happens. The narrator cares too.

There is very occasional mild humorous understatement — when the Tin Woodman weeps at Dorothy's departure "in a way most dangerous to his joints" — but this is the comic relief of someone who genuinely loves the material, not the detachment of someone who finds it absurd.

---

## Narrator Position

**Frame Narrator: Implied storytelling presence** [INFERRED]

Baum's framing (the Introduction, the dedication) positions the narrator as an author-figure consciously telling a story to an implied child audience. The narrator is not quite a character, but not fully invisible either — they are present as a presence, a voice that chose to tell this particular story in this particular way. The choice to eliminate "all horrible and blood-curdling incidents" is a narrator decision, not just an author decision. The narrator is clean-handed by design.

---

## Voice Differentiation Note

Despite the omniscient access, each character speaks in their own register:

- **Dorothy:** Direct, earnest, practical, homesick undertone throughout
- **Scarecrow:** Philosophically cheerful, playful about his own inadequacy, curious
- **Tin Woodman:** Formal, emotionally cautious, careful about the weight of what he says
- **Cowardly Lion:** Self-deprecating, verbose about his shame, dramatic
- **Wizard:** Grandiose, circular, ultimately deflating
- **Glinda:** Measured, warm, knowing without being withholding (she ultimately tells Dorothy the truth)

The narrator renders these voice differences through dialogue primarily, not through interior mode-switching. Each character's inner life is reported in the narrator's consistent voice — but the things they feel are calibrated to their specific type.
